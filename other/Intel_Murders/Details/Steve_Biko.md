# Steve Biko
South African anti-apartheid activist and founder of the Black Consciousness Movement, beaten to death in police custody in 1977. Five officers later confessed to killing him but were denied amnesty.

## Assessment: CONFIRMED

Five former Security Branch police officers confessed in 1997 to having killed Steve Biko and applied for amnesty from the Truth and Reconciliation Commission. Amnesty was denied in 1999. The apartheid government's original inquest found no one responsible, a ruling universally condemned as a cover-up. In 2025, South Africa's National Prosecuting Authority reopened the inquest into Biko's death, 48 years after the killing.

## Circumstances of Death

On August 18, 1977, Biko and fellow activist Peter Jones were stopped at a roadblock near Grahamstown and detained under Section 6 of the Terrorism Act, which allowed indefinite detention without trial. Biko was taken to the Walmer police station in Port Elizabeth and held in the Security Branch offices at the Sanlam Building. During interrogation on September 6 or 7, Biko sustained a severe head injury. Police claimed he had "banged his head against a wall" during a scuffle, but evidence later showed he was severely beaten. Despite visible signs of brain injury — including foam around his mouth and inability to speak coherently — medical attention was delayed for days. On September 11, Biko was loaded naked and shackled into the back of a Land Rover and driven over 740 miles (1,190 km) from Port Elizabeth to Pretoria. He was found lying on the floor of a cell in the Pretoria Central Prison and died the following day, September 12, 1977, from a massive brain hemorrhage.

## Background

Steve Biko was born in King William's Town in the Eastern Cape. He studied medicine at the University of Natal before founding the South African Students' Organisation (SASO) in 1968 and becoming the leading voice of the Black Consciousness Movement, which sought to instill psychological liberation and pride among Black South Africans. He was expelled from university for his activism and placed under a banning order in 1973 that restricted his movements, associations, and speech. Despite the ban, he continued organizing community programs, health clinics, and political education. By 1977, he was considered one of the most dangerous opponents of the apartheid regime.

## Intelligence Connections

* The Security Branch of the South African Police — the apartheid state's political police — detained, interrogated, and killed Biko
* Colonel Pieter Goosen led the interrogation team; other officers involved included Captain Daantjie Siebert, Warrant Officer Rubin Marx, Detective Sergeant Niewoudt, and Harold Snyman
* Biko was the 46th person to die in South African police detention, part of a systematic pattern of custodial killings
* The Security Branch regularly used torture during interrogations of political detainees, as documented extensively by the TRC
* The apartheid government's initial cover-up — including false claims by Minister of Justice Jimmy Kruger that Biko died from a hunger strike — demonstrated state-level coordination to conceal the killing

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- The original 1977 inquest magistrate found that "no one was responsible" for Biko's death, a ruling widely regarded as a whitewash
- Police Minister Jimmy Kruger initially told parliament that Biko died from a hunger strike and publicly joked that "it leaves me cold"
- Biko was driven 740 miles naked and shackled while suffering from catastrophic brain injuries, rather than being taken to the nearest hospital
- Two doctors who examined Biko — Dr. Ivor Lang and Dr. Benjamin Tucker — failed to diagnose his obvious brain injury or insist on hospitalization; they were later found guilty of improper conduct
- Despite the 1997 confessions, no one has ever been criminally prosecuted for Biko's murder
- The 2025 reopening of the inquest, 48 years later, underscores that justice has never been served
