## Assessment: CONFIRMED

This assassination has been thoroughly documented through criminal proceedings in Malaysian courts. North Korean intelligence operatives recruited two women to apply VX nerve agent to Kim Jong-nam's face, then fled the country. The operation bore all hallmarks of a state-directed assassination using a banned chemical weapon. Kim Jong-nam was reportedly also a CIA informant, adding a layer of intelligence rivalry to the killing.

## Circumstances of Death

On 13 February 2017, at approximately 9:00 a.m., Kim Jong-nam was waiting at a self-check-in kiosk at KLIA2 (the low-cost carrier terminal) for a flight to Macau. Two women — Siti Aisyah from Indonesia and Doan Thi Huong from Vietnam — approached him and smeared VX nerve agent on his face. Kim Jong-nam sought help at an airport information counter, complaining that someone had grabbed his face and sprayed something. He died within 15 to 20 minutes while being transported to the hospital. Notably, Kim Jong-nam was carrying a vial of atropine — the antidote to VX — in his bag at the time of his death, suggesting he was aware he might be targeted.

## Background

Kim Jong-nam was the eldest son of North Korean dictator Kim Jong-il by his mistress Song Hye-rim. From roughly 1994 to 2001, he was considered the heir apparent to his father's regime. He fell out of favor in 2001 after being caught trying to enter Japan on a fraudulent Dominican Republic passport to visit Tokyo Disneyland. He was effectively exiled around 2003 and lived abroad in Macau, Singapore, and Malaysia, becoming an occasional critic of the regime. In 2019, the Wall Street Journal reported that Kim Jong-nam had been a CIA informant, meeting with CIA operatives on multiple occasions.

## Intelligence Connections

* Four North Korean operatives reportedly provided the two women with the VX agent and then fled Malaysia on the day of the murder
* North Korea's Reconnaissance General Bureau, its primary intelligence agency, allegedly directed the operation
* Kim Jong-nam was reportedly a CIA source, according to the Wall Street Journal and a book by Washington Post journalist Anna Fifield
* He was reportedly seen with a US intelligence agent and carried $120,000 in cash in a backpack
* VX nerve agent is a banned chemical weapon; North Korea has not ratified the Chemical Weapons Convention and allegedly maintains stockpiles

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- The use of VX nerve agent — one of the deadliest substances known — in a crowded international airport demonstrated extraordinary boldness
- The two women claimed they were tricked into participating, believing they were part of a prank TV show
- Kim Jong-nam's dual role as both a potential regime rival and an alleged CIA asset gave multiple parties potential motives
- Malaysian authorities identified and sought four North Korean suspects who fled the country immediately after the attack
- North Korea denied any involvement and demanded the return of the body, refusing to identify the victim as Kim Jong-nam
- The killing effectively served as a chemical weapons attack on foreign soil
- The four North Korean suspects were never apprehended, having departed on commercial flights the same day
- The two women used as proxies were victims of deception — charges against Aisyah were dropped, and Huong was convicted of a lesser charge and released in 2019
- North Korea demanded the return of the body and refused to identify the victim as Kim Jong-nam
- Malaysia expelled the North Korean ambassador and severed diplomatic ties over the incident
