## Assessment: SUSPICIOUS

Haney was a DHS whistleblower who had publicly alleged that the Obama administration ordered the deletion of intelligence records on Islamic extremist networks. He authored the book *See Something, Say Nothing* detailing these claims and was reportedly preparing additional disclosures at the time of his death. While the Amador County Sheriff's Office ruled the death a suicide in 2022 — citing a suicide note, forensic handwriting analysis, and witness statements about his recent depression — family members have publicly stated they "seriously doubt" the finding, noting Haney's devout Christian faith and statements that he would never take his own life.

## Circumstances of Death

On February 21, 2020, Philip Haney was found dead in a park-and-ride lot along State Highway 16 near its intersection with State Highway 124 in Amador County, east of Sacramento, California. He died from a single gunshot wound. A firearm was found at the scene.

The Amador County Sheriff's Office investigated for two years before ruling the death a suicide in February 2022. The investigation found a suicide note with a signature that forensic analysis determined was in Haney's handwriting. Investigators also reported that a neighbor said Haney "appeared depressed lately" and had given the neighbor his potted plants the day before his death. Personal and financial paperwork was reportedly arranged neatly on his kitchen counter, some containing instructions for asset distribution.

## Background

Philip Haney was a founding member of the Department of Homeland Security when it was established in 2002, initially serving as a Customs and Border Protection agriculture officer. He later became a terrorism analyst at the National Targeting Center, where he compiled information that reportedly led to the identification of hundreds of suspected terrorists, earning multiple awards and commendations.

Haney became a prominent whistleblower, alleging that the Obama-era DHS ordered him to delete intelligence records linking members of Islamic organizations to terrorist networks. He retired from DHS in July 2015 and co-authored the book *See Something, Say Nothing: A Homeland Security Officer Exposes the Government's Submission to Jihad* (2016) with Art Moore. The book became a bestseller in conservative circles.

At the time of his death, Haney was reportedly engaged to be married and had told associates he was preparing to reveal additional information about terrorism-related intelligence suppression.
