## Assessment: HIGHLY SUSPICIOUS

Perepilichnyy was a key whistleblower in the Magnitsky fraud case who had fled Russia fearing for his life. He collapsed suddenly while jogging, and initial tests found traces of Gelsemium — a rare plant-based poison historically associated with assassinations. However, Surrey Police discarded most of his stomach contents, lost forensic computer evidence, and failed to realize he was a whistleblower for weeks. A coroner ultimately ruled natural causes, but the investigation was so compromised that a definitive conclusion was impossible. US intelligence reportedly assessed his death as an assassination "with high confidence."

## Circumstances of Death

On November 10, 2012, Perepilichnyy collapsed while jogging near his home on Granville Road in St. George's Hill, Weybridge — one of England's most exclusive gated communities. He had returned from a trip to Paris the previous night, during which he reportedly vomited repeatedly. He was pronounced dead at the scene.

Surrey Police initially treated the death as non-suspicious. It took weeks before they realized Perepilichnyy was a whistleblower connected to a massive Russian fraud case. By then, critical evidence had been compromised.

## Background

Perepilichnyy was a Russian financier who fled to the UK in 2009 after beginning to provide information about a $230 million Russian treasury fraud to Hermitage Capital Management CEO Bill Browder. This was the same fraud that Russian tax advisor Sergei Magnitsky had exposed before being arrested and beaten to death in Russian custody in 2009. Perepilichnyy provided Swiss authorities with evidence identifying Russian officials involved in the money laundering, contributing to the passage of the US Magnitsky Act in 2012.

He reportedly told associates he was too afraid to return to Russia and had taken out life insurance policies worth millions of pounds.
