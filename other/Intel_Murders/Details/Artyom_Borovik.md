## Assessment: SUSPICIOUS

Artyom Borovik was one of Russia's most prominent investigative journalists, and his publications were openly critical of Vladimir Putin. His last investigation focused on the 1999 Russian apartment bombings — which he and others alleged had been orchestrated by the FSB to justify the Second Chechen War. He died in a plane crash under circumstances that, while officially attributed to pilot error and icing conditions, struck many observers as suspiciously timed.

## Circumstances of Death

On March 9, 2000, Borovik boarded a chartered Yakovlev Yak-40 at Sheremetyevo International Airport bound for Kyiv. The plane was chartered by Chechen oil industry executive Ziya Bazhayev. All nine people on board — four passengers and five crew — perished when the aircraft crashed shortly after takeoff.

The official investigation by the Interstate Aviation Committee found that snow was removed from the aircraft exterior but de-icing fluid was not applied. The crew reportedly did not ask for permission to enter the taxiway, taxied at too high a speed for icy conditions, and set the flaps to 11 degrees instead of the required 20 degrees.

## Background

Borovik was the son of Genrikh Borovik, a prominent Soviet journalist. He gained international recognition working for CBS's *60 Minutes* during the 1990s and became one of Russia's most well-known investigative journalists. He published the monthly investigative newspaper *Top Secret* and hosted the television program *Versiya*, both of which tackled corruption, organized crime, and political scandals.

His *Top Secret* TV program and print publications were openly critical of Vladimir Putin. His final investigation reportedly focused on the Russian apartment bombings of September 1999, in which explosions in Moscow and other cities killed nearly 300 people. Borovik and other investigators alleged these bombings were orchestrated by the FSB as a pretext for the Second Chechen War — the same investigation that would later cost journalist Anna Politkovskaya and former FSB officer Alexander Litvinenko their lives.
