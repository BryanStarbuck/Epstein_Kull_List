## Assessment: HIGHLY SUSPICIOUS

Fidan Doğan was a Kurdish political activist murdered alongside [Sakine Cansiz](Sakine_Cansiz.md) and [Leyla Saylemez](Leyla_Saylemez.md) at the Kurdistan Information Center in Paris. French investigators reportedly concluded that members of Turkish intelligence (MIT) were involved in the triple assassination. Some analysts have speculated that Doğan may have been the primary target due to her political connections in France and her role bridging Kurdish, Turkish, and French networks.

## Circumstances of Death

During the night of January 9-10, 2013, three Kurdish women were found dead inside the Kurdistan Information Center at 147 Rue La Fayette, Paris. All three had been shot multiple times in the head and neck at close range. Security camera footage from the building showed Turkish national Ömer Güney entering the premises between 00:11 and 00:56 — the time window of the killings. Güney was arrested after being identified on surveillance footage. He denied involvement.

## Background

Fidan Doğan worked at the Kurdistan Information Center in Paris and also served as the French representative of the Brussels-based Kurdistan National Congress (KNK). A representative of the Peoples' Democratic Party (HDP) speculated that Doğan may have been the primary target of the triple assassination because of her social and political connections in France. A lawyer described her as "very clever" and noted she had "three cultures: French, Turkish and Kurdish" — making her uniquely effective as a political intermediary. She was deeply involved in Kurdish advocacy work in Europe and had built significant relationships with French political figures.

## Intelligence Connections

* French investigators reportedly concluded that members of MIT were involved in the triple assassination
* Suspect Ömer Güney was allegedly in contact with MIT handlers — recordings of these conversations were published in Turkish media
* A document resembling a MIT "mission order" was reportedly leaked to the media
* Güney died of brain cancer in a Paris prison in December 2016, days before his trial was scheduled to begin
* MIT officially denied any role in 2014
* After Güney's death, the Paris prosecutor closed the case, but an anti-terror judge was later appointed to investigate further following complaints from the victims' families

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- Execution-style killings with multiple shots to the head indicate a professional operation, not a crime of passion
- Doğan's unique role bridging Kurdish, Turkish, and French political networks made her a high-value target for Turkish intelligence
- The sole suspect died before trial, preventing testimony that could have exposed the chain of command
- French investigators' conclusion pointing to MIT involvement has not led to charges against any intelligence officials
- The timing during Turkish-PKK peace negotiations suggests the killings may have been designed to sabotage diplomatic progress
- Turkey's NATO membership complicated France's ability to pursue the intelligence angle aggressively
