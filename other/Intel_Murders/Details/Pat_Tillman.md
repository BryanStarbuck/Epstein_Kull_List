## Assessment: HIGHLY SUSPICIOUS

Pat Tillman was shot three times in the forehead at close range by members of his own unit. Army doctors who examined the body told investigators the wounds suggested murder, stating "the medical evidence did not match up with the scenario as described." The Pentagon initially reported Tillman died heroically fighting the enemy and used his death as pro-war propaganda, concealing the friendly fire for five weeks. Army attorneys sent congratulatory emails for keeping criminal investigators away. A three-star general was censured for lying to investigators. Tillman had become critical of the Iraq War and had reportedly planned to meet with Noam Chomsky upon his return.

## Circumstances of Death

On April 22, 2004, Tillman's platoon was split into two elements for a patrol in eastern Afghanistan's Khost Province. Tillman's group was in a canyon when a firefight erupted. Members of the second element, positioned on higher ground, began firing into the canyon — at their own men.

Tillman and an Afghan militia fighter (AMF) were waving their arms, yelling, and even popping smoke to identify themselves as friendly forces. Despite these efforts, the firing continued. The AMF soldier was killed first. Tillman was then struck by three rounds to the forehead — a tight grouping at close range — from an M249 Squad Automatic Weapon (SAW).

Army medical examiners who examined the body noted that the three bullet holes in Tillman's forehead were closely grouped and could have been made from approximately 10 yards away. According to medical personnel, "the medical evidence did not match up with the scenario as described" by the soldiers involved.

After Tillman's death, soldiers were ordered to burn his uniform and body armor — standard evidence in a wrongful death investigation. His personal diary was also reportedly burned.

## Background

Pat Tillman was a safety for the Arizona Cardinals of the NFL. After the September 11, 2001 attacks, he turned down a three-year, $3.6 million contract extension to enlist in the U.S. Army with his brother Kevin in May 2002. They both served as Army Rangers and were deployed to Iraq and Afghanistan.

Tillman was, by multiple accounts, becoming deeply critical of the Iraq War, which he reportedly described as "so f***ing illegal." He had become interested in the writings of Noam Chomsky and had allegedly arranged to meet Chomsky after his return from deployment. He kept a personal diary documenting his evolving views.
