# Yitzhak Rabin
Israeli Prime Minister shot and killed by far-right extremist Yigal Amir at a peace rally in Tel Aviv on November 4, 1995. Allegations persist that the Shin Bet domestic intelligence service failed to prevent the assassination despite operating an agent provocateur, Avishai Raviv, within the extremist milieu surrounding the assassin.

## Assessment: MODERATE SUSPICION

The assassination itself is not in question — Yigal Amir shot Rabin and was convicted. The intelligence dimension concerns the Shin Bet's operation of agent provocateur Avishai Raviv (codename "Champagne") within the far-right extremist movement that produced the assassin. Raviv was close to Amir's circles and allegedly incited violence against Rabin, yet the Shin Bet failed to detect or prevent the assassination plot. A court acquitted Raviv of charges that he failed to prevent the killing, finding no evidence he knew of Amir's specific plan. The Shamgar Commission found the Shin Bet agent was "not properly controlled." Whether this represents negligence or something more deliberate remains a matter of debate.

## Circumstances of Death

On the evening of November 4, 1995, Prime Minister Rabin attended a massive peace rally in support of the Oslo Accords at Kings of Israel Square (now Rabin Square) in Tel Aviv. An estimated 100,000 people attended the rally.

As Rabin descended the steps toward his armored car at approximately 9:30 p.m., Yigal Amir approached from behind and fired two shots with a Beretta 84F semi-automatic pistol, hitting Rabin in the chest and abdomen. During the ensuing struggle, Amir fired a third shot that lightly wounded bodyguard Yoram Rubin. Rabin was rushed to nearby Ichilov Hospital, where he died approximately 40 minutes later from blood loss and a punctured lung.

Rabin had refused requests to wear a bulletproof vest and had declined to use the armored car that had been purchased for his protection, reportedly preferring his regular vehicle.

## Background

Yitzhak Rabin was one of Israel's most consequential leaders. He served as Chief of Staff of the Israel Defense Forces during the 1967 Six-Day War, as Ambassador to the United States, and twice as Prime Minister (1974-1977 and 1992-1995). His second term was defined by the Oslo Accords — the historic peace agreement with the Palestine Liberation Organization that earned Rabin, Shimon Peres, and Yasser Arafat the 1994 Nobel Peace Prize.

The Oslo process was fiercely opposed by Israel's far right, including settler movements and ultranationalist groups who viewed any territorial concessions as a betrayal. Rallies against Rabin featured protesters carrying coffins and posters depicting him in an SS uniform or in crosshairs. The incitement campaign was intense and prolonged.

## Intelligence Connections

* The Shin Bet operated Avishai Raviv (codename "Champagne") as an agent/informer within the far-right extremist movement
* Raviv was reportedly one of the most extreme voices in the movement, allegedly inciting violence against Rabin, including distributing flyers and organizing provocative demonstrations
* Raviv had connections to Yigal Amir's extremist circles at Bar-Ilan University
* The Shamgar Commission, which investigated the assassination, found that Raviv was "not properly controlled" by his Shin Bet handlers
* Israeli Finance Minister Bezalel Smotrich claimed at a Rabin memorial event in 2023 that the Shin Bet "encouraged" the assassination — a claim that Amir himself contradicted, stating the Shin Bet "didn't know anything" about his plot
* Former Shin Bet heads accused Benjamin Netanyahu of lying about the lead-up to the assassination
* Raviv was charged with failing to prevent the assassination but was acquitted — the court found no evidence he knew of Amir's specific plan

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- The Shin Bet had an agent provocateur operating within the exact extremist milieu that produced the assassin
- Raviv allegedly incited violence against Rabin while on the Shin Bet's payroll
- The Shamgar Commission found the Shin Bet agent was "not properly controlled"
- Rabin's security detail failed to prevent a gunman from approaching the Prime Minister at close range in a known threat environment
- Rabin was not wearing a bulletproof vest despite the extreme threat level
- The broader question of whether the Shin Bet's failure was negligence or something more intentional has never been definitively resolved
- The political environment created by the incitement campaign — which Raviv contributed to — directly enabled the assassination
