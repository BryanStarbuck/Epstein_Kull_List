## Assessment: CONFIRMED

South African Military Intelligence officer Craig Williamson confessed to ordering and carrying out the letter bomb attack that killed Ruth First. Williamson applied for and was granted amnesty by the Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) in 2000 for First's murder. This is a confirmed state-sponsored assassination by the apartheid regime's intelligence apparatus.

## Circumstances of Death

On August 17, 1982, Ruth First was at the Centre for African Studies at Eduardo Mondlane University in Maputo, Mozambique, where she served as research director. She opened a parcel bomb that had been sent to her through the mail. The explosion killed her instantly. Three others in the room — centre director Aquino de Braganca, lecturer Bridget O'Laughlin, and visiting ANC activist Pallo Jordan — were injured but survived.

## Background

Ruth First was born in Johannesburg to Jewish Lithuanian immigrant parents who were founding members of the South African Communist Party (SACP). She became a prominent journalist, academic, and anti-apartheid activist. She was a member of the SACP and the ANC, and was married to Joe Slovo, who later became head of the SACP and commander of Umkhonto we Sizwe (MK), the ANC's armed wing. First was detained under the 90-Day Detention Law in 1963 and held in solitary confinement for 117 days, an experience she documented in her book *117 Days*. After her release, she went into exile, first in London and then in Mozambique, where she continued her academic work on migrant labor, agrarian reform, and southern African politics.

## Intelligence Connections

* Major Craig Williamson of the South African Security Branch personally orchestrated the letter bomb operation
* Williamson was a deep-cover agent who had infiltrated anti-apartheid organizations, including the International University Exchange Fund in Geneva
* The bomb was reportedly assembled by explosives expert Brigadier Piet Goosen of the Security Branch
* The apartheid government's intelligence services maintained a systematic program of targeting exiled activists through letter bombs, car bombs, and cross-border raids
* Williamson was granted amnesty by the TRC in 2000 after confessing to the killing, confirming this was an authorized state operation
* Joe Slovo, First's husband, was reportedly the primary target; the bomb may have been intended for him

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- While the perpetrators are confirmed, the full chain of command authorizing the assassination has never been fully disclosed
- Williamson received amnesty despite the premeditated nature of the killing, a decision that remains controversial
- The apartheid regime's letter bomb program killed multiple exiled activists, representing a systematic campaign of extraterritorial assassination
- First's assassination was part of a broader pattern of South African intelligence targeting academics, journalists, and activists in neighboring states
- The bombing injured three other people, demonstrating indifference to collateral casualties
- Williamson continued to live freely in South Africa after receiving amnesty, a source of ongoing anguish for First's family
- The same letter bomb techniques were used in the attempted assassination of ANC lawyer Bheki Mlangeni and the killing of Jeanette Schoon and her six-year-old daughter Katryn in 1984
