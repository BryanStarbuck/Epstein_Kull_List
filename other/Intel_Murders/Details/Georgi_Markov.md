## Assessment: CONFIRMED

The assassination was carried out by the Bulgarian secret police (Darzhavna Sigurnost) with technical assistance and the ricin weapon provided by the KGB. Multiple high-ranking KGB defectors, including Oleg Kalugin and Oleg Gordievsky, confirmed KGB involvement. The attack took place on the birthday of Bulgarian dictator Todor Zhivkov, who reportedly ordered the assassination. The weapon — a modified umbrella that fired a ricin-filled pellet — became one of the most infamous assassination devices in espionage history.

## Circumstances of Death

On September 7, 1978, Georgi Markov was waiting for a bus on Waterloo Bridge in London during his daily commute to the BBC World Service, where he worked as a journalist and broadcaster. A man approached him from behind and jabbed him in the back of the right thigh with the tip of a modified umbrella. The man apologized in a foreign accent, hailed a taxi, and disappeared.

Markov felt a sharp stinging pain and noticed a small red mark on his thigh. He mentioned the incident to colleagues at the BBC. That evening, he developed a high fever. Within hours, his condition deteriorated rapidly. He was admitted to St. James's Hospital in Balham, where doctors were unable to determine the cause of his illness.

Markov died three days later, on September 11, 1978. During the post-mortem examination, a tiny spherical pellet — approximately 1.52mm in diameter, made of 90% platinum and 10% iridium — was recovered from his thigh. The pellet contained two precisely drilled holes forming an X-shaped cavity, in which traces of ricin were found. The poison had been sealed in the pellet with a sugary substance designed to melt at body temperature, releasing the ricin into his bloodstream.

## Background

Georgi Markov was one of Bulgaria's most celebrated writers and playwrights. Born in Sofia in 1929, he became a prominent literary figure in communist Bulgaria, winning prizes for his novels and plays. However, his increasingly frank portrayals of life under communism brought him into conflict with the regime.

In 1969, Markov defected to the West, eventually settling in London. He worked as a journalist and broadcaster for the BBC World Service, Radio Free Europe, and Deutsche Welle. His radio broadcasts back to Bulgaria, particularly his series "Reports on Bulgaria" (In Absentia Reports), were devastating critiques of the communist regime that reached millions of Bulgarian listeners.

Markov's broadcasts were considered so damaging that Bulgarian dictator Todor Zhivkov reportedly made multiple requests to the KGB for assistance in silencing him. At least two previous assassination attempts against Markov had allegedly failed.

## Intelligence Connections

- **KGB technical assistance:** The KGB provided the assassination weapon (the modified umbrella) and the ricin pellet to Bulgarian intelligence. The weapon was developed at the KGB's secret Laboratory 12 (also known as the "Kamera" or Chamber)
- **Bulgarian DS execution:** The Bulgarian Darzhavna Sigurnost carried out the operation on orders from the highest levels of the Bulgarian government
- **KGB defector confirmation:** Oleg Kalugin, former head of KGB foreign counterintelligence, confirmed KGB involvement in a 1991 interview. Oleg Gordievsky, a KGB defector, provided further corroboration
- **Todor Zhivkov's birthday:** The attack was carried out on September 7, Zhivkov's birthday, reportedly as a "gift" to the dictator
- **The assassin:** Francesco Gullino (codename "Agent Piccadilly"), an Italian smuggler recruited by Bulgarian intelligence, was identified as the likely triggerman. He was never charged
- **Related attack:** Ten days before Markov's assassination, Bulgarian dissident Vladimir Kostov was attacked in Paris with the same method but survived because the pellet's sugar coating failed to fully dissolve

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- The case was one of the first documented state-sponsored assassinations using a sophisticated biological weapon
- The platinum-iridium pellet was an engineering marvel designed to be virtually undetectable
- The KGB's "Kamera" laboratory had been developing assassination poisons since the Stalin era
- The identified assassin, Francesco Gullino, was never prosecuted despite being identified by multiple investigations
- Bulgaria's intelligence archives were partially destroyed after 1989, eliminating key evidence
- Scotland Yard's investigation was reopened in 2008 but closed without charges due to insufficient evidence for a prosecution
- The case established the template for future Russian intelligence poisonings — exotic toxins, plausible deniability, targeting of defectors abroad
