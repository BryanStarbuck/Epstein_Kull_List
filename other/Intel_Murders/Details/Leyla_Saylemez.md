## Assessment: HIGHLY SUSPICIOUS

Leyla Saylemez was the youngest victim of the 2013 Paris triple assassination, killed alongside [Sakine Cansiz](Sakine_Cansiz.md) and [Fidan Doğan](Fidan_Dogan.md). French authorities reportedly found evidence suggesting Turkish intelligence involvement. The sole suspect died before trial, and the case has not been fully resolved despite family complaints and an appointed anti-terror judge.

## Circumstances of Death

During the night of January 9-10, 2013, Saylemez was found dead alongside Cansiz and Doğan inside the Kurdistan Information Center at 147 Rue La Fayette in the 10th arrondissement of Paris. All three women had been shot multiple times in the head and neck at close range — execution-style. Security cameras recorded suspect Ömer Güney, a 34-year-old Turkish national employed as a maintenance worker at Paris Charles de Gaulle Airport, entering the building between 00:11 and 00:56 — the estimated time of the killings. Güney was subsequently arrested.

## Background

Leyla Saylemez was born in 1988 in Mersin, Turkey, to a Yazidi family originally from the Lice district of Diyarbakır. Her family moved to Germany in the 1990s, where she grew up. She had been studying architecture for one year before she joined the Kurdish freedom movement. At just 24, she was the youngest of the three victims and was active in the Kurdish youth movement in Europe. She had traveled to Paris in connection with Kurdish political activities at the time of her murder.

## Intelligence Connections

* French authorities reportedly found evidence suggesting MIT involvement in the triple assassination
* Recordings allegedly of conversations between suspect Ömer Güney and two Turkish intelligence handlers were published in Turkish media
* A leaked document reportedly resembled a MIT "mission order" for the operation
* Güney died of brain cancer in his Paris prison cell on December 17, 2016, just before his trial was set to begin
* After Güney's death, the French prosecutor closed the investigation, but the victims' families successfully petitioned for an anti-terror judge to continue the probe
* MIT officially denied any role in the killings

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- The execution-style killing of three women — including a 24-year-old student — indicates a professional intelligence operation, not a personal crime
- Saylemez's youth and relatively junior position suggest she was collateral to the operation targeting Cansiz and/or Doğan, making her death especially troubling
- The sole suspect's death from brain cancer days before trial eliminated the only path to testimony about who ordered the killings
- The French investigation's apparent conclusion of MIT involvement has not resulted in charges against any intelligence officials
- The timing during sensitive Turkish-PKK peace negotiations raises questions about whether the killings were intended to derail diplomacy
- Thousands marched in Paris in subsequent years demanding justice, but the case remains unresolved
