## Assessment: HIGHLY SUSPICIOUS

Roman Tsepov's death by radioactive poisoning in 2004 was a precursor to the more widely known Litvinenko poisoning in 2006. His symptoms were nearly identical — rapid decline, hair loss, organ failure. Only state-level intelligence services are believed to have had access to the radioactive materials used. The St. Petersburg prosecutor's office classified the case as premeditated murder, but no one has ever been charged.

## Circumstances of Death

Tsepov fell seriously ill on September 11, 2004. Over the following two weeks, his condition deteriorated rapidly in a pattern that would become grimly familiar after the Litvinenko case — progressive organ failure, hair loss, and a collapsing immune system. He died on September 24, 2004.

A postmortem investigation confirmed poisoning by an unspecified radioactive material. The exact toxin has been disputed, with theories ranging from radioactive elements to thallium derivatives or unidentified heavy metals. The mode of administration was never determined.

## Background

In November 1992, Tsepov founded "Baltic Escort," which grew into the largest private security firm in St. Petersburg. The firm provided protection to the city's most powerful officials, including Mayor Anatoly Sobchak and his family, as well as Vice-Mayor Vladimir Putin. Tsepov reportedly had a close personal relationship with Putin during the 1990s, serving as his de facto bodyguard.

Tsepov's influence extended far beyond personal security. He was connected to Russia's most powerful figures, including Minister of Internal Affairs Rashid Nurgaliyev, Presidential Security Service chief Viktor Zolotov (who attended Tsepov's funeral), and deputy head of presidential administration Igor Sechin. He reportedly served as an intermediary between organized crime and the political elite in St. Petersburg.

## Intelligence Connections

* Tsepov provided personal security to Putin when Putin was vice-mayor of St. Petersburg — placing him at the heart of the security establishment
* His connections to Zolotov, Sechin, and Nurgaliyev indicate deep ties to Russia's intelligence and security apparatus
* Only Russian security services (FSB, GRU) are believed to have had access to radioactive materials capable of the poisoning
* His death preceded Litvinenko's polonium-210 poisoning by almost exactly two years, using a strikingly similar method
* The OCCRP documented his role as "Putin's top bodyguard" and his extensive connections in St. Petersburg's power structures

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- Radioactive poisoning strongly implies a state-level actor — private individuals do not have access to such materials
- His symptoms were nearly identical to those of Alexander Litvinenko, who was confirmed to have been killed by the FSB
- Despite classification as premeditated murder, no one has ever been charged
- Tsepov knew intimate details of Putin's early career in St. Petersburg and the intersection of politics, intelligence, and organized crime
- His role as intermediary between power structures may have made him a liability once his associates rose to national power
- The precise toxin has never been publicly identified, raising questions about whether evidence was suppressed
