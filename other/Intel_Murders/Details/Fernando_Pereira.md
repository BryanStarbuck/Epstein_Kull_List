## Assessment: CONFIRMED

France officially acknowledged responsibility for the bombing of the Rainbow Warrior. The operation, codenamed "Operation Satanique," was carried out by agents of the Direction Generale de la Securite Exterieure (DGSE), France's foreign intelligence service. Two DGSE agents were arrested, tried, and convicted of manslaughter. French Defence Minister Charles Hernu resigned, and DGSE director Admiral Pierre Lacoste was dismissed.

## Circumstances of Death

On the night of July 10, 1985, two limpet mines were attached to the hull of the Rainbow Warrior while it was moored at Marsden Wharf in Auckland Harbour. The first explosion at 11:38 PM drove most crew members onto the wharf. Pereira went back below deck to retrieve his camera equipment. The second, larger explosion — designed to sink the vessel — detonated minutes later, causing a massive inrush of water. Pereira was trapped below deck and drowned. He was the only fatality.

## Background

Fernando Pereira was born in Chaves, Portugal. As a young man, he fled Portugal to avoid conscription into the dictator Salazar's colonial war in Angola. He traveled on foot and by hitchhiking through Spain to the Netherlands, where he settled, married a Dutch woman, and pursued photography. He joined Greenpeace as a freelance photographer and was aboard the Rainbow Warrior to document French nuclear testing at Moruroa Atoll in the South Pacific. The ship was preparing to lead a protest flotilla against the tests.

## Intelligence Connections

* The DGSE planned and executed Operation Satanique on direct orders from the French government to prevent the Rainbow Warrior from disrupting nuclear tests at Moruroa Atoll
* DGSE agents Major Alain Mafart and Captain Dominique Prieur were arrested by New Zealand police and convicted of manslaughter in November 1985
* French Defence Minister Charles Hernu authorized the operation and resigned when it was exposed
* DGSE Director Admiral Pierre Lacoste was fired after the scandal became public
* France initially denied involvement, then was forced to admit responsibility after a government inquiry led by Bernard Tricot
* A team of DGSE frogmen planted the explosives; other agents provided logistical support from within New Zealand

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- A NATO ally conducted a state-sponsored terrorist attack in a friendly nation's harbour, killing a civilian
- France initially mounted a disinformation campaign denying any involvement
- The convicted agents served less than two years of their ten-year sentences after France pressured New Zealand through trade sanctions and threats
- France paid $8.16 million in compensation but never fully accounted for all agents involved in the operation
- The operation demonstrated that Western intelligence services were willing to kill civilians to protect nuclear weapons programs
- French President Mitterrand reportedly approved the operation, though this was never officially confirmed

## Aftermath

New Zealand expelled France's ambassador and demanded a formal apology. The two convicted agents, Mafart and Prieur, were sentenced to ten years in prison but were transferred to the French military base on Hao Atoll in the Pacific under a 1986 agreement brokered by the United Nations. Both were returned to France well before their sentences expired — Mafart after just over a year on Hao, and Prieur after two years. Both received promotions upon their return to France. France paid $8.16 million in compensation to New Zealand and $2.3 million to Pereira's family. The scandal became the biggest political crisis of the Mitterrand presidency. In 2005, on the 20th anniversary, the surviving DGSE team leader publicly apologized to Pereira's family.
