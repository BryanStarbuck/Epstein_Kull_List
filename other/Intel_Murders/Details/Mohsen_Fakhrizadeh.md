# Mohsen Fakhrizadeh
Iran's chief nuclear scientist, assassinated in a road ambush near Tehran in 2020 using a remote-controlled, AI-assisted machine gun operated by satellite. The operation is widely attributed to Israel's Mossad and represented a new frontier in autonomous targeted killing technology.

## Assessment: CONFIRMED

Israel has effectively acknowledged responsibility for the assassination of Mohsen Fakhrizadeh. Former Mossad chief Yossi Cohen offered Israel's closest public admission in a June 2021 television interview, all but confirming the operation. The New York Times and other outlets reported in detail on the remote-controlled weapon system used, based on intelligence sources. The operation demonstrated an unprecedented level of technological sophistication in targeted killing.

## Circumstances of Death

On 27 November 2020, Fakhrizadeh was traveling in a convoy of vehicles on a highway near the town of Absard, about 60 kilometers east of Tehran. A Nissan pickup truck was parked on the side of the road with a specially modified weapon concealed in its bed.

As Fakhrizadeh's car passed the truck, a remotely operated machine gun — a modified Belgian-made FN MAG — opened fire. The weapon was mounted on a robotic apparatus equipped with artificial intelligence that could compensate for time delay and the movement of the target vehicle. According to reports, the weapon was operated via satellite by a team of Mossad agents watching from a distance, possibly from outside Iran.

Fakhrizadeh was hit by multiple rounds and died shortly afterward. His wife, sitting in the same car, was reportedly unharmed. The weapon system self-destructed via a built-in explosive charge after the attack, destroying evidence. No assassins were present at the scene — the entire operation was conducted remotely.

## Background

Fakhrizadeh was a brigadier general in the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps and was widely regarded as the architect of Iran's nuclear weapons program. He headed the Physics Research Center and later the Organization of Defensive Innovation and Research (SPND), which Western intelligence agencies alleged was a front for nuclear weapons development.

In 2018, Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu publicly identified Fakhrizadeh during a presentation on Iran's nuclear program, displaying a slide that read: "Remember that name." The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) had identified him as the leader of Iran's "Project Amad," a program allegedly aimed at developing nuclear weapons capability.

- The assassination violated Iranian sovereignty and international law regarding targeted killings

- The precedent of autonomous, AI-assisted assassination weapons raises profound ethical and legal questions about the future of targeted killing
