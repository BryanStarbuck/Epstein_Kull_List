## Assessment: HIGHLY SUSPICIOUS

The death of John Paisley is one of the most bizarre cases in CIA history. A senior CIA officer who worked on the most sensitive Soviet intelligence analyses disappeared from his sailboat and was found floating with a gunshot wound and a weighted dive belt — yet the body was four inches shorter and thirty pounds lighter than Paisley. The FBI's fingerprint records for Paisley had been "inexplicably lost," and the CIA arranged for cremation before the family could make a positive identification. The Senate Intelligence Committee investigated for two years but kept its findings secret.

## Circumstances of Death

On September 24, 1978, Paisley set sail alone on his 31-foot sloop *Brillig* on the Chesapeake Bay. He radioed the Coast Guard that evening but was never heard from again. His boat was found adrift the next day. On October 1, a body was found floating near the mouth of the Patuxent River with a 9mm gunshot wound behind the left ear and two CIA-issued scuba diving weight belts strapped around the waist.

The body was badly decomposed from immersion. It was identified as Paisley's despite being approximately four inches shorter and thirty pounds lighter than his known measurements. No member of Paisley's family viewed the body before it was cremated. To obtain fingerprints, the FBI severed both hands and peeled back layers of decomposing skin — but the prints could not be compared because the FBI reported that Paisley's original fingerprints, taken when he was hired by the CIA in 1953, had been "inexplicably lost" from their files.

## Background

Paisley was a career CIA officer who served for over 25 years, primarily in the Directorate of Intelligence. He was the CIA's deputy director of the Office of Strategic Research and was deeply involved in analyzing Soviet strategic capabilities. He worked on some of the most sensitive intelligence assessments of the Cold War, including the "Team B" competitive analysis exercise of 1976, which challenged CIA estimates of Soviet military strength.

By 1978, Paisley had officially retired from the CIA but remained active as a contractor. He was reportedly still engaged in highly classified work related to Soviet intelligence analysis. His disappearance came during a period of intense controversy within the intelligence community over Soviet capabilities and the Team B findings.

## Intelligence Connections

* Paisley was a senior CIA officer for over 25 years with access to the most classified Soviet intelligence assessments
* He was involved in the controversial "Team B" exercise, which had major implications for US defense policy
* According to various investigations, allegations emerged that Paisley may have been a Soviet mole — though this was never proven
* The CIA's handling of the body identification and rapid cremation raised questions about institutional cover-up
* The FBI's loss of his original fingerprint records eliminated the primary means of identification
* The Senate Select Committee on Intelligence conducted a two-year investigation but kept its full report secret

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- The body was four inches shorter and thirty pounds lighter than Paisley's known measurements
- FBI fingerprint records taken at CIA hiring in 1953 had been "inexplicably lost"
- No family member viewed the body before cremation, which the CIA arranged
- The weighted dive belt suggested either suicide preparation (unusual) or deliberate sinking of a body
- A 9mm gunshot behind the left ear is an unusual angle for self-inflicted wounds
- The Senate Intelligence Committee investigated for two years but kept its findings classified
- The CIA's rapid arrangement of cremation prevented any future identification efforts
- Theories ranged from suicide, to murder by Soviet intelligence, to Paisley being a Soviet agent who faked his death and defected
- His sailboat contained sophisticated radio equipment that the CIA never adequately explained
