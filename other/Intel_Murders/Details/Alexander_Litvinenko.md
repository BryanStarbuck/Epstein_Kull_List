## Assessment: CONFIRMED

The European Court of Human Rights concluded that Russia was responsible for Litvinenko's assassination. The 2016 UK public inquiry, led by Sir Robert Owen, found that the operation was "probably approved by Mr. Patrushev, then head of the FSB, and also by President Putin." Two former Russian intelligence agents, Andrey Lugovoy and Dmitry Kovtun, were identified as the assassins.

## Circumstances of Death

On November 1, 2006, Litvinenko met with Andrey Lugovoy and Dmitry Kovtun at the Pine Bar of the Millennium Hotel in Mayfair, London. During the meeting, polonium-210 was administered to Litvinenko in a cup of green tea. He fell ill almost immediately and was hospitalized.

Over the following three weeks, Litvinenko's condition deteriorated dramatically. His hair fell out, his organs failed, and he suffered intense pain. Doctors initially struggled to identify the cause, with thallium poisoning initially suspected. Only in the final hours of his life was polonium-210 identified as the toxin.

Litvinenko died on November 23, 2006, at University College Hospital. The Po-210 used to poison him was later traced to the Avangard nuclear facility in Sarov, Russia, with the initial irradiation of bismuth carried out at the Mayak facility in Ozersk.

Investigators found an intensely radioactive teapot at the Millennium Hotel with "off-the-charts" readings for polonium-210. A trail of radioactive contamination was traced across multiple locations in London that Lugovoy and Kovtun had visited.

## Background

Alexander Litvinenko served as an officer of the Russian Federal Security Service (FSB) and its predecessor, the KGB. In November 1998, he made international headlines when he and several colleagues held a televised press conference accusing the FSB of ordering the assassination of oligarch Boris Berezovsky.

After the press conference, Litvinenko was arrested, imprisoned, and eventually acquitted. He fled Russia with his family in late 2000, settling in London where he was granted political asylum. In exile, he worked as a consultant for the British intelligence service MI6 and became a vocal critic of Vladimir Putin and the Russian government.

Litvinenko co-authored the book *Blowing Up Russia: Terror from Within*, which accused the FSB of orchestrating the 1999 Russian apartment bombings that killed nearly 300 people — bombings that were used as justification for the Second Chechen War and helped propel Putin to the presidency. He also investigated links between the Russian government and organized crime.

## Intelligence Connections

- The UK public inquiry (2016) concluded the assassination was "probably approved by Mr. Patrushev, then head of the FSB, and also by President Putin"
- The European Court of Human Rights ruled in September 2021 that Russia was responsible, stating it "cannot but conclude" that Lugovoy and Kovtun were "acting as agents of the respondent State"
- Andrey Lugovoy was a former member of Russia's Federal Protective Service (FSO); Dmitry Kovtun had connections to Russian military intelligence
- The polonium-210 was produced at Russian state nuclear facilities
- Litvinenko had been working as a consultant for MI6 at the time of his death
- The FSB had a documented motive: Litvinenko's public accusations about the apartment bombings, his cooperation with Western intelligence, and his investigations into Russian organized crime

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- Polonium-210 is an extraordinarily rare and expensive poison, accessible almost exclusively to state actors with nuclear capabilities
- The method of assassination — radioactive poisoning — left a trail that could be (and was) traced back to Russia, suggesting it was intended as a message
- Russia refused to extradite Lugovoy, citing constitutional prohibition on extraditing Russian citizens
- Lugovoy was subsequently elected to the Russian Duma, giving him parliamentary immunity
- Multiple previous attempts on Litvinenko's life had reportedly been made
- The assassination came shortly after Litvinenko had been investigating the murder of journalist Anna Politkovskaya (killed October 7, 2006)
