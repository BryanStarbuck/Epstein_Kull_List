# Zelimkhan Yandarbiyev
Former president of the Chechen Republic of Ichkeria, assassinated by a car bomb in Doha, Qatar in 2004 — one of the few confirmed cases where Russian military intelligence (GRU) agents were caught, tried, and convicted of an extraterritorial assassination.

## Assessment: CONFIRMED

This is one of the most clear-cut cases of a state-sponsored assassination by Russian intelligence. Two GRU agents were arrested, tried, and convicted by a Qatari court, which found they had acted on orders from then-Russian Defense Minister Sergei Ivanov. The agents were sentenced to life imprisonment but were repatriated to Russia under diplomatic pressure and received a heroes' welcome in Moscow. They were never imprisoned in Russia.

## Circumstances of Death

On February 13, 2004, Yandarbiyev left a mosque in the Qatari capital after Friday prayers with his 13-year-old son Daud. A bomb detonated under his SUV as he drove away. Yandarbiyev was fatally wounded and died en route to the hospital. His son Daud was critically injured but survived.

Qatari authorities moved quickly. Within days, they arrested three Russians operating from a villa connected to the Russian Embassy. One suspect, first secretary Aleksandr Fetisov, was released due to diplomatic immunity. The remaining two — identified as GRU agents Anatoly Yablochkov (also known as Belashkov) and Vasily Pugachyov — were charged with the assassination.

## Background

Yandarbiyev served as acting president of the Chechen Republic of Ichkeria from 1996 to 1997, following the assassination of Dzhokhar Dudayev. He was a Chechen poet, politician, and independence activist who had been one of the founders of the Chechen independence movement. After the Second Chechen War began in 1999, Yandarbiyev went into exile in Qatar.

Russia had designated Yandarbiyev as a terrorist and accused him of financing Chechen insurgents. He was placed on the UN Security Council's terrorism sanctions list in 2003 at Russia's request.

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- The Qatari court explicitly stated the agents "acted on orders from the Russian leadership"
- After sentencing to life imprisonment, diplomatic pressure forced Qatar to repatriate the agents to Russia in December 2004
- The agents received a heroes' welcome upon returning to Moscow and disappeared from public view
- Russian prison authorities admitted in February 2005 that the agents were not in prison, stating the Qatari sentence was "irrelevant" in Russia
- The case demonstrated that Russia would carry out assassinations on foreign soil and then use diplomatic leverage to protect its operatives
- Yandarbiyev's 13-year-old son was critically injured in the attack, showing willingness to accept civilian casualties
