## Circumstances of Death

On the night of September 17-18, 1961, a chartered Transair Sweden DC-6B aircraft (the Albertina) carrying Hammarskjold and 15 others crashed in a forest approximately 9 miles from Ndola airport in Northern Rhodesia. Hammarskjold was traveling to negotiate a ceasefire in the Congo Crisis with Moise Tshombe, the leader of the breakaway province of Katanga.

The aircraft was on final approach to Ndola when it went down. Fifteen of the sixteen people aboard were killed; the sole survivor, Sergeant Harold Julien, died six days later. Julien reportedly told nurses before dying that there had been an explosion aboard the aircraft.

The bodies showed unusual injuries. Hammarskjold himself had an ace of spades tucked into his collar -- a detail that has never been satisfactorily explained.

## Background

Hammarskjold served as the second Secretary-General of the United Nations from 1953 until his death. He was a Swedish diplomat widely regarded as one of the most effective leaders the UN has ever had. President John F. Kennedy called him "the greatest statesman of our century."

His aggressive pursuit of Congolese independence and opposition to Western mining interests in Katanga put him in direct conflict with powerful forces. The Belgian mining company Union Miniere had vast copper and uranium interests in Katanga and was backing Tshombe's secession. Western intelligence agencies -- particularly the CIA and MI6 -- were deeply involved in the Congo Crisis, supporting various factions.

## Intelligence Connections

- Documents discovered in 1998 detailed an alleged plot named "Operation Celeste" to assassinate Hammarskjold, backed by the CIA, MI6, and Belgian mining interests. One document stated that CIA director Allen Dulles agreed that "Dag is becoming troublesome.. and should be removed"
- The South African Institute for Maritime Research (SAIMR), a white militia group with intelligence connections, allegedly orchestrated the crash with support from British and American intelligence
- At least one MI6 official was present at Ndola for approximately six days around the time of the crash, involved in a "top-secret mission"
- An NSA officer stationed at a listening post in Cyprus claimed he intercepted radio transmissions of a fighter pilot opening fire on Hammarskjold's plane
- South African Truth and Reconciliation Commission documents included a letter claiming a bomb was placed in the aircraft's wheel bay

## Why This Death Raises Questions
