## Assessment: SUSPICIOUS

William Colby, the CIA Director who shocked the intelligence establishment by cooperating with congressional investigations and revealing the CIA's "Family Jewels" — a catalogue of illegal CIA activities — was found dead under circumstances that left his family and colleagues divided. He left a half-eaten dinner on the table, his computer on, and lights still burning in his weekend home before allegedly going on a solo canoe trip on a cold April evening. His canoe was found the next day on a sandbar; his body was found nine days later. The medical examiner ruled he suffered a cardiovascular event and drowned. The timing, circumstances, and Colby's status as someone who had made powerful enemies within the intelligence community have fueled enduring suspicion.

## Circumstances of Death

On the evening of Saturday, April 27, 1996, Colby was alone at his weekend home in Rock Point, Maryland, on the Wicomico River (a tributary of the Potomac). Sometime that evening, he apparently went out in his canoe.

When his wife, Sally Shelton-Colby, could not reach him by phone on Sunday morning, she asked neighbors to check. They found the house with lights on, a half-eaten dinner on the table, a glass of wine still poured, and his computer running — as if he had suddenly left in the middle of his evening routine. His canoe was missing.

On Monday, April 29, the canoe was found partially submerged on a sandbar approximately a quarter mile from his home. There was no sign of Colby.

On May 6, nine days after his disappearance, Colby's body was found face-down in a marshy area of the riverbank, not far from where the canoe had been discovered. He was fully clothed.

Maryland's Chief Medical Examiner, Dr. John E. Smialek, ruled the death accidental. The autopsy found "severe calcified atherosclerosis" and concluded that Colby likely suffered a heart attack or stroke while canoeing, fell into the cold water, and succumbed to hypothermia and drowning.

## Background

William Colby was a Princeton-educated lawyer who served in the Office of Strategic Services (OSS) during World War II, parachuting behind enemy lines in France and Norway. He joined the CIA at its founding and rose through the ranks, serving in Vietnam where he directed the controversial Phoenix Program (a counterinsurgency operation accused of widespread assassination of Viet Cong operatives).

Colby served as Director of Central Intelligence from September 1973 to January 1976 — one of the most turbulent periods in CIA history. In a decision that enraged many colleagues, Colby cooperated extensively with the Church Committee and the Pike Committee, providing Congress with the "Family Jewels" — a secret internal catalogue of CIA illegalities including assassination plots, domestic surveillance, and illegal drug experiments (MKULTRA).

Colby personally apologized to Frank Olson's family for the CIA's role in Olson's death under MKULTRA. His willingness to expose CIA abuses made him deeply unpopular within the agency. He was fired by President Ford in November 1975 and replaced by George H.W. Bush.

After leaving the CIA, Colby practiced law in Washington and remained engaged in intelligence and foreign policy discussions.

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- Colby left a half-eaten dinner, wine glass poured, computer running, and lights on — suggesting he left abruptly or was interrupted
- Going out for a solo canoe trip on a cold April evening after dark is unusual behavior
- His body was not found for nine days despite the canoe being located within a day
- Colby had made powerful enemies within the intelligence community by exposing CIA crimes
- He was the most senior CIA official to ever cooperate with congressional oversight
- His son Carl Colby suggested in a documentary that his father may have committed suicide from guilt — but Colby's wife, other family members, and his biographer rejected this theory
- No investigation beyond the initial autopsy was conducted
- The medical examiner's finding of cardiovascular disease provided a plausible natural explanation, but did not explain the unusual circumstances of his departure from the house
