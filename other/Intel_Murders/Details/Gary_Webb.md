## Assessment: HIGHLY SUSPICIOUS

Gary Webb wrote the "Dark Alliance" series exposing how the CIA's Contra-connected drug trafficking network flooded American cities with crack cocaine. The CIA's own internal report later confirmed much of what Webb reported. Yet Webb's career was systematically destroyed by a coordinated media campaign — the CIA's own records show the agency actively managed the media response. Webb was found dead with two gunshot wounds to the head. While two-shot suicides are medically possible, they are extremely rare. The Sacramento County coroner ruled the death a suicide, noting a handwritten note and evidence at the scene.

## Circumstances of Death

On December 10, 2004, Gary Webb was found dead in his home in Carmichael, California, a suburb of Sacramento. He had two gunshot wounds to the head from a .38-caliber revolver. According to the Los Angeles Times, the first shot went through his face, entering near his right ear and exiting at his left cheek. The second shot struck an artery and was fatal.

Sacramento County Coroner Robert Lyons ruled the death a suicide. When questioned about the unusual nature of two gunshots in a suicide, Lyons stated: "It's unusual in a suicide case to have two shots, but it has been done in the past, and it is in fact a distinct possibility." The coroner's report indicated that Webb had left a handwritten note.

At the time of his death, Webb had recently sold his house, was being forced to move, and had been unable to find full-time journalism work since the "Dark Alliance" controversy. He had been working as an investigator for the California State Legislature.

## Background

Gary Webb was a Pulitzer Prize-winning investigative journalist who worked for the San Jose Mercury News. In August 1996, he published "Dark Alliance," a three-part series that traced the origins of the crack cocaine epidemic in Los Angeles to a CIA-connected Nicaraguan drug trafficking network.

Webb's investigation focused on a West Coast drug ring led by Oscar Danilo Blandon and Norwin Meneses, who sold cocaine to "Freeway" Ricky Ross, the largest crack dealer in South-Central Los Angeles. The profits from the cocaine sales were funneled to the CIA-backed Contra rebels fighting the Sandinista government in Nicaragua.

The series created a firestorm. Congresswoman Maxine Waters and other members of the Congressional Black Caucus demanded investigations. The CIA launched an internal review.

## Intelligence Connections

* **The CIA actively managed the media destruction of Webb's career.** Declassified CIA documents obtained by The Intercept show the agency's public affairs office tracked the "Dark Alliance" story and worked to influence media coverage against Webb.
* A CIA internal memo stated the goal was "ichacking down" (a CIA term) the story by working with journalists at the Washington Post, New York Times, and Los Angeles Times.
* The Washington Post, New York Times, and Los Angeles Times attacked Webb's reporting, sometimes based on claims Webb never actually made.
* In **1998, CIA Inspector General Frederick Hitz released two reports** that confirmed the CIA had failed to investigate or act on allegations that Contra forces it supported were involved in drug trafficking — effectively vindicating Webb's core findings.
* The Kerry Committee (Senate Foreign Relations Committee), led by then-Senator John Kerry, had reached similar conclusions in 1989.
* Despite the CIA Inspector General's findings, Webb received no rehabilitation. His career was already destroyed.
