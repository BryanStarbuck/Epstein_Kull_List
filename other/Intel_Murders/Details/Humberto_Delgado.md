## Assessment: CONFIRMED

A Portuguese court convicted PIDE agent Casimiro Monteiro in absentia in 1981 for the murder of Delgado. The operation was authorized by the Portuguese dictator Antonio de Oliveira Salazar, who, when informed of the killing, reportedly responded simply, "Uma macada" ("Such a bother"). PIDE's original claim that Monteiro acted in self-defense was contradicted by the fact that Delgado was unarmed and his secretary was strangled.

## Circumstances of Death

On February 13, 1965, Delgado and his Brazilian secretary, Arajaryr Moreira de Campos, were lured into an ambush near the Spanish border town of Villanueva del Fresno. Delgado had been living in exile and was attempting to clandestinely enter Portugal. PIDE agent Casimiro Monteiro shot and killed Delgado, and then strangled his secretary de Campos. PIDE subsequently claimed the original plan was an extraordinary rendition — Delgado was to be kidnapped and brought back to Portugal for trial — and that Monteiro acted contrary to orders by killing him. Their bodies were not discovered until approximately two months later, near the Spanish village.

## Background

Humberto Delgado was a Portuguese Air Force general who became the leading opposition figure against the Salazar dictatorship. A decorated military officer, he had served as Portugal's military attache to Washington, D.C. and held the rank of general in the Portuguese Air Force. In the 1958 Portuguese presidential election, he ran against the regime's candidate with the rallying cry "Obviously I'll sack him" — referring to Salazar. Despite widespread belief that Delgado won the popular vote, the regime declared its candidate the winner in a fraudulent count. Delgado was subsequently stripped of his military rank and forced into exile, living in Brazil and later Algeria. He continued to organize opposition to the Estado Novo regime from abroad, attempting to coordinate military uprisings and building international support for democratic change in Portugal. His persistence and international visibility made him a constant thorn in Salazar's side and a persistent target for PIDE surveillance and operations.

## Intelligence Connections

* PIDE (International and State Defence Police) was the Portuguese secret police under the Salazar dictatorship
* The assassination was authorized at the highest levels — reportedly by Salazar himself
* PIDE agent Casimiro Monteiro carried out the killing; he was the same operative later implicated in the assassination of Eduardo Mondlane in 1969
* The operation was conducted on Spanish territory with apparent acquiescence from Franco's Spain
* PIDE maintained extensive networks of informants and agents among Portuguese exile communities

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- Delgado was unarmed when killed, contradicting PIDE's claim of self-defense
- His secretary was strangled — a deliberate killing inconsistent with any self-defense scenario
- The bodies were hidden and not found for approximately two months
- The same PIDE agent (Casimiro Monteiro) was later implicated in the assassination of Eduardo Mondlane in Mozambique
- The operation was conducted on foreign soil (Spain), indicating coordination between Portuguese and Spanish security services
- Salazar's dismissive reaction to the news suggests the killing was expected and authorized
- The 1981 conviction of Monteiro was in absentia — he was never physically brought to justice
