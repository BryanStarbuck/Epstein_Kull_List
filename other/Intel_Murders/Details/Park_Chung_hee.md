## Assessment: SUSPICIOUS

Park was assassinated by his own intelligence chief at a private dinner, making this a confirmed intelligence service killing. The deeper question is whether U.S. intelligence had advance knowledge or played a facilitating role. CIA station chief Robert Brewster had met with assassin Kim Jae-gyu just weeks before the killing, and Brewster reportedly identified Kim as the killer by the following day. The Carter administration's strained relationship with Park over human rights abuses and the Koreagate scandal adds a geopolitical dimension to the assassination.

## Circumstances of Death

On the evening of October 26, 1979, President Park attended a private dinner at a KCIA safe house in the Gungjeong-dong district of Seoul. Present were KCIA Director Kim Jae-gyu, presidential chief security officer Cha Ji-chul, and presidential secretary Kim Gye-won, among others.

During the dinner, Kim Jae-gyu drew a Walther PPK pistol and shot Cha Ji-chul, then turned and shot President Park in the chest. Kim left the room briefly, obtained a second pistol from an aide, returned, and shot Park again — this time fatally — in the head. Kim Jae-gyu also killed four of Park's bodyguards that evening. Park was pronounced dead at a nearby Army hospital.

Kim Jae-gyu was arrested, tried by military tribunal, convicted, and executed by hanging on May 24, 1980. He claimed the assassination was a patriotic act to restore democracy.

## Background

Park Chung-hee seized power in a military coup in 1961 and ruled South Korea as an authoritarian president for 18 years. Under his leadership, South Korea underwent rapid industrialization, but his regime was marked by brutal suppression of political opposition, press censorship, and human rights abuses. In 1972, he declared martial law and enacted the Yushin Constitution, effectively making himself president for life.

By 1979, Park faced mounting domestic unrest, including the Busan-Masan uprising (Bu-Ma Democratic Protests), mass protests against his authoritarian rule. His relationship with the United States had deteriorated due to the Koreagate bribery scandal and the Carter administration's focus on human rights.

## Intelligence Connections

* The assassin, Kim Jae-gyu, was the sitting Director of the KCIA — South Korea's most powerful intelligence agency
* U.S. CIA station chief Robert Brewster met with Kim Jae-gyu on multiple occasions, including a meeting in September 1979 during rising tensions between Park and his critics
* U.S. Ambassador William Gleysteen also met with Kim Jae-gyu in the weeks preceding the assassination
* By October 27 — one day after the killing — CIA station chief Brewster had reportedly concluded that the plot was hatched by the KCIA director
* The speed of Brewster's conclusion has raised questions about whether the CIA had advance intelligence about Kim's intentions
* A 2019 lawsuit sought CIA records related to U.S. involvement in the assassination, alleging foreknowledge
