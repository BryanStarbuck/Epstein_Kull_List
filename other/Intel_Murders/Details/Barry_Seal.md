## Assessment: HIGHLY SUSPICIOUS

Barry Seal was a commercial pilot who became one of the most prolific drug smugglers in American history, operating a fleet of aircraft from Mena, Arkansas. After his arrest, he became a DEA informant and participated in a CIA-backed sting operation against the Medellin Cartel. His cover was allegedly blown when Oliver North and the Reagan administration leaked surveillance photos to the media. A federal judge then sentenced Seal to a halfway house with no protection — making him a sitting duck. The Cartel hit squad that killed him was easy to find; the question is why Seal was left so exposed by the agencies he served.

## Circumstances of Death

On the evening of February 19, 1986, Barry Seal pulled his white Cadillac into the parking lot of the Salvation Army Community Treatment Center in Baton Rouge, Louisiana, where he was serving a court-ordered sentence. Two Colombian hitmen — Luis Carlos Quintero-Cruz and Miguel Velez — ambushed him and opened fire with MAC-10 submachine guns, killing him in his car.

Seal was unarmed. As a condition of his sentence, he was required to be at the halfway house every night by 6 PM and was forbidden from carrying weapons or employing bodyguards. He had repeatedly pleaded with the judge to change the arrangements, warning that the Medellin Cartel had placed a $500,000 bounty on his head (with $1 million if captured alive). The judge refused.

Three hitmen — Quintero-Cruz, Velez, and Bernardo Antonio Vasquez — were convicted of first-degree murder and sentenced to life imprisonment without parole. They were connected to the Medellin Cartel through Pablo Escobar's organization.

## Background

Barry Seal was a former TWA pilot from Baton Rouge who became the most prolific drug smuggler of his era, reportedly importing $3-5 billion worth of cocaine into the United States. He operated primarily from Mena Intermountain Municipal Airport in rural Arkansas, using a fleet of aircraft to transport cocaine from Colombia and weapons to the Contra rebels in Nicaragua.

After his arrest on drug smuggling charges in 1983, Seal agreed to become a DEA informant. He participated in a CIA-backed sting operation in which he flew to Nicaragua and photographed Sandinista officials loading cocaine onto his plane. The photographs were intended to prove that the Nicaraguan government was involved in drug trafficking.

In 1984, Seal flew a specially equipped C-123K cargo plane with hidden CIA cameras to photograph Pablo Escobar and other cartel leaders loading cocaine. The resulting photos were given to the DEA and CIA.

## Intelligence Connections

* **CIA drug flights from Mena, Arkansas:** An FBI, Arkansas State Police, and IRS investigation confirmed that Seal used the Mena airport for "smuggling activity" from late 1980 through March 1984. The CIA's Inspector General confirmed in 1996 that the CIA ran a "joint training operation with another federal agency at Mena Intermountain Airport."
* **Contra weapons supply:** Seal reportedly transported weapons to the Contra rebels in Nicaragua on behalf of CIA-connected operations. His planes allegedly carried cocaine on the return flights.
* **Oliver North's role:** Iran-Contra figure Oliver North attended meetings about the sting operation involving Seal. The Kerry Committee (Senate Foreign Relations Committee) pinned the leak of Seal's identity as an informant on North, stating he "decided to play politics with the issue."
* **Blown cover:** After the CIA surveillance photos were leaked to the media, the Medellin Cartel identified Seal as a DEA informant. The leak effectively signed his death warrant.
* **Unprotected sentence:** Despite credible death threats and a $500,000 cartel bounty, a federal judge sentenced Seal to serve time at a halfway house with no security. Seal was forbidden from carrying weapons or hiring bodyguards.
* **All charges dropped:** According to the Senate Foreign Relations Committee, all charges and cases related to Barry Seal and others connected to the Mena airport were dropped due to "potential national security risks."

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- Seal's cover as a DEA informant was allegedly blown by Oliver North and the Reagan administration for political purposes
- A federal judge placed Seal in a completely unprotected halfway house despite known cartel death threats
- Seal had knowledge of CIA-Contra drug operations that could have been devastating to the Reagan administration if made public
- All criminal cases connected to the Mena airport operations were dropped for "national security" reasons
- The CIA confirmed conducting operations at Mena but has never fully disclosed their scope
- Seal's death silenced the most dangerous witness to CIA drug trafficking connections
- The cartel hired the hitmen, but the U.S. government created the conditions that made the assassination possible
