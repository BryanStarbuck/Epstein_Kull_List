# Oscar Romero

## Assessment: CONFIRMED

The United Nations Truth Commission for El Salvador concluded that Major Roberto D'Aubuisson "gave the order to assassinate the Archbishop" and that military officers coordinated the killing. D'Aubuisson was a graduate of the U.S. School of the Americas and maintained close ties with Salvadoran military intelligence. Declassified U.S. documents revealed that the Reagan and George H.W. Bush administrations knew of D'Aubuisson's role in death squad killings, including Romero's assassination, yet continued working with him. In 2004, a U.S. federal court found Captain Alvaro Saravia liable for the killing.

## Circumstances of Death

On March 24, 1980, Archbishop Romero was celebrating Mass at the chapel of the Hospital de la Divina Providencia in San Salvador. As he finished his homily and stood at the altar, a red Volkswagen Passat pulled up outside the chapel entrance. A gunman stepped to the door and fired a single shot — reportedly from a high-powered rifle — that struck Romero in the chest. He collapsed behind the altar and bled to death. The assassin escaped in the vehicle. Just the day before, on March 23, Romero had delivered his most famous sermon, broadcasting a direct appeal to soldiers of the Salvadoran army: "I beg you, I implore you, I order you in the name of God: stop the repression!"

## Background

Oscar Romero was ordained as a priest in 1942 and appointed Archbishop of San Salvador in 1977. Initially considered a conservative, safe choice by the Salvadoran elite, Romero was transformed by the murder of his friend Father Rutilio Grande by government-linked death squads in 1977. He became the most prominent voice for human rights in El Salvador, using his weekly radio homilies to document massacres, disappearances, and torture by the military and its allied death squads. His sermons reached virtually the entire country and drew international attention to the escalating violence. He was canonized as a saint by the Catholic Church in 2018.

## Intelligence Connections

* Major Roberto D'Aubuisson, who ordered the assassination, was trained in intelligence and communications at the U.S. School of the Americas in 1972
* D'Aubuisson ran death squads including the "White Warriors Union" and his personal operation codenamed "One by One," which targeted priests
* Declassified CIA and State Department documents (approximately 12,000 pages) revealed that U.S. administrations knew about D'Aubuisson's death squad activities as early as 1980-1981 but continued to support the Salvadoran military government
* The CIA reportedly had intelligence about the assassination plot but did not act to prevent it
* Captain Alvaro Saravia, a Salvadoran military officer, organized the logistics of the killing and was found liable in a 2004 U.S. federal court case brought under the Alien Tort Claims Act
* The Salvadoran military was receiving significant U.S. military aid and training at the time of the assassination

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- The U.S. government knew D'Aubuisson was running death squads yet continued to support the Salvadoran military, raising questions about complicity
- Despite the UN Truth Commission findings, no one was ever criminally convicted in El Salvador for Romero's murder
- D'Aubuisson went on to found the ARENA political party and ran for president, continuing to operate with U.S. acquiescence
- Romero had written to President Jimmy Carter weeks before his death, pleading for the U.S. to stop military aid to El Salvador; Carter did not respond meaningfully
- At Romero's funeral on March 30, 1980, snipers fired into the crowd of 250,000 mourners, killing dozens — another atrocity attributed to the military
- The School of the Americas trained numerous officers linked to the worst atrocities documented by the UN Truth Commission
