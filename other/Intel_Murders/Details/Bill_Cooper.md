# Milton William "Bill" Cooper
Author, radio broadcaster, and self-described whistleblower who exposed alleged government conspiracies in his bestselling book *Behold a Pale Horse* (1991). Shot and killed by Apache County Sheriff's deputies during a late-night arrest operation at his home in Eagar, Arizona, on November 5, 2001 — less than two months after the September 11 attacks he had warned about.

## Assessment: SUSPICIOUS

Bill Cooper's death resulted from a direct confrontation with law enforcement, and he did shoot a deputy in the head during the encounter — facts that complicate any straightforward "silencing" narrative. However, the timing raises questions: Cooper was killed less than two months after the 9/11 attacks that he had publicly predicted on his radio show in June 2001, and the federal arrest warrant for tax evasion had been outstanding for over three years before authorities chose to act. The decision to execute a tactical nighttime operation against a known armed recluse — rather than continue the patient approach that had held since 1998 — invites scrutiny about whether the goal was arrest or elimination.

## Circumstances of Death

On the night of November 5, 2001, shortly before midnight, the Apache County Sheriff's Special Response Team initiated a tactical plan to arrest Cooper at his hilltop home in Eagar, Arizona. The operation involved approximately 17 officers.

Two sheriff's deputies, disguised as civilians in a pickup truck, positioned themselves near Cooper's property to lure him away from his house and his stockpile of weapons. Cooper drove down to confront them rather than approaching on foot as anticipated. When the deputies identified themselves as law enforcement, Cooper drove back toward his house.

According to official accounts, Cooper nearly struck an officer when he swerved around a roadblock erected in front of his home. As he exited his vehicle and ran toward his house, Cooper fired on the deputies. Deputy Rob Marinez was shot twice in the head and critically wounded. Deputy Joseph Goldsmith returned fire, mortally wounding Cooper before he could reach his front door.

Deputy Marinez was airlifted to Barrow Neurological Institute in Phoenix. Cooper was pronounced dead at the scene.

## Background

### Military Service

Cooper served in both the United States Air Force and the United States Navy. In the Air Force, he held a secret clearance working on B-52 bombers, KC-135 refueling aircraft, and Minuteman missiles before receiving an honorable discharge in 1965.

In the Navy, Cooper served aboard multiple vessels and postings, including the submarine USS *Tiru* (SS-416), Naval Support Activity Danang, Naval Security and Intelligence at Camp Carter in Vietnam, Dong Ha River Security Group, and USS *Oriskany* (CVA-34). He claimed to have served on the Intelligence Briefing Team for the Commander in Chief of the Pacific Fleet (CINCPACFLT) at Makalapa, Hawaii, where he reportedly held a Top Secret, Q, SI security clearance and was designated a KL-47 SPECAT operator in the CINCPACFLT Command Center.

After his death, questions were raised about Cooper's claims of extensive involvement with naval intelligence. Some researchers noted that his enlisted rank (E-5, Petty Officer Second Class) would not typically grant access to the level of classified material he described. However, his service records do confirm naval service including postings at intelligence-related facilities in Vietnam.

### *Behold a Pale Horse* (1991)

Cooper's book became one of the most influential conspiracy texts in American history. It presented allegations of CIA drug trafficking, secret government plans for population control, a hidden global elite, connections to the JFK assassination, and government foreknowledge of various events. Political scientist Michael Barkun characterized it as "among the most complex superconspiracy theories" and "among the most influential."

The book sold hundreds of thousands of copies and reportedly became the number-one bestseller in the American prison system. It was widely read in Black communities and urban bookstores, and was referenced by numerous hip-hop artists including Wu-Tang Clan, Tupac Shakur, Jay-Z, and Prodigy of Mobb Deep. Its influence crossed ideological lines, reaching right-wing militia groups, hip-hop culture, and eventually feeding into modern conspiracy movements including QAnon.

### *The Hour of the Time* Radio Show (1992–2001)

From 1992 until his death, Cooper broadcast his shortwave radio show *The Hour of the Time* from a studio in his home in Eagar, Arizona. The show covered government conspiracies, constitutional law, militia movement issues, and intelligence service operations.

### The 9/11 Prediction

On June 28, 2001 — roughly ten weeks before the September 11 attacks — Cooper told his radio audience during a broadcast about a CNN interview with Osama bin Laden that a "major attack" on a large U.S. city was imminent and that it would be "blamed on Osama bin Laden," adding, "but don't you believe it." This broadcast has been widely cited as one of the most specific public warnings issued before the attacks.

On September 11, 2001, Cooper broadcast for hours, and he predicted the U.S. would soon be at war in "two or maybe three countries." He also began promoting the controlled demolition theory on the day of the attacks, which later became central to the 9/11 truth movement.

### Legal Troubles and Federal Targeting

In July 1998, Cooper was charged with federal tax evasion and bank fraud. An arrest warrant was issued, but federal authorities chose not to serve it immediately, reportedly hoping to avoid a violent confrontation with the heavily armed Cooper, who had declared he would not be taken alive.

Cooper had become increasingly convinced that he was being personally targeted by the Clinton administration and the Internal Revenue Service. He stopped leaving his property, barricaded his hilltop home, and warned listeners that any attempt to arrest him would end in a shootout.

The standoff between Cooper and federal authorities lasted over three years. Then, in July 2001, a separate state warrant was issued by Apache County after Cooper allegedly left his property and threatened a passerby with a firearm, resulting in charges of aggravated assault with a deadly weapon and endangerment.

### FBI Surveillance

The FBI maintained files on Cooper, which have been partially released. The bureau monitored his broadcasts and activities, particularly as his influence within the militia movement grew in the late 1990s. Cooper was named by President Clinton's White House as "the most dangerous radio host in America," according to Cooper's own claims on his broadcast.

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- **Timing:** Cooper was killed less than two months after the 9/11 attacks — an event he had specifically predicted on his radio show in June 2001. His continued broadcasting about government foreknowledge and cover-ups made him a prominent voice of dissent during the most sensitive period in modern American security history.

- **Three-year warrant suddenly enforced:** Federal authorities had held an arrest warrant for tax evasion since 1998 and deliberately chose not to serve it to avoid violence. After 9/11, the calculus suddenly changed. The state aggravated assault charge from July 2001 provided a new legal basis for the operation.

- **Nighttime tactical operation:** The arrest was executed shortly before midnight using a 17-officer tactical team with plainclothes decoys — an approach more consistent with a high-risk military operation than a routine warrant service on a 58-year-old man with a disability (Cooper had lost a leg in a prior incident).

- **No negotiation attempt documented:** Unlike other standoff situations with armed subjects (such as Ruby Ridge or Waco, which involved extended negotiation phases), there is no public record of authorities attempting to negotiate Cooper's peaceful surrender before launching the tactical operation.

- **Cooper's prior warnings:** Cooper had repeatedly told his audience that if he was killed, it would not be an accident. He stated on air that he expected to be murdered by the government and that his death should be investigated.

- **His book's influence:** *Behold a Pale Horse* was one of the bestselling conspiracy texts in American history, reaching millions of readers. Cooper's elimination removed one of the most prominent anti-government voices at the exact moment the government was consolidating unprecedented new powers through the Patriot Act (signed October 26, 2001 — ten days before Cooper's death).

- **Post-9/11 climate:** The weeks after 9/11 saw an aggressive crackdown on dissenting voices. Cooper was broadcasting daily about government complicity in the attacks. His killing occurred during a period when challenging the official narrative was being equated with being unpatriotic or even dangerous.

## Counterpoints

- Cooper did shoot Deputy Marinez twice in the head, critically wounding him. This is well-documented and makes a straightforward "assassination" theory difficult to sustain.
- Cooper had made explicit threats that he would not surrender peacefully and would use lethal force against anyone who attempted to arrest him.
- The aggravated assault charge (threatening a passerby with a gun) was a state matter, not federal, suggesting local law enforcement had independent reasons to act.
- Cooper's increasingly erratic behavior in his final years — including threatening neighbors — had made him a genuine safety concern for the local community.
- Some of Cooper's claims about his intelligence background have not been verified, raising questions about the reliability of his insider knowledge.

## Legacy

Cooper is often described as "the original conspiracy theorist" or the "godfather of conspiracy theories." His work predated and influenced Alex Jones, the 9/11 truth movement, QAnon, and much of modern conspiracy culture. Mark Jacobson's 2018 biography *Pale Horse Rider: William Cooper, the Rise of Conspiracy, and the Fall of Trust in America* documented Cooper's influence on American culture.

Cooper's death — whether a justified shooting of a dangerous fugitive or the elimination of a dissident voice — remains a subject of debate. What is not debated is his influence: *Behold a Pale Horse* remains in print and continues to sell steadily more than three decades after publication.
