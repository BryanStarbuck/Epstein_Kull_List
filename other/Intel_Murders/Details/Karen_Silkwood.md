## Assessment: HIGHLY SUSPICIOUS

Karen Silkwood was driving to deliver documents proving safety violations at the Kerr-McGee plutonium plant to a New York Times reporter when her car left the highway and struck a concrete culvert wall. The Oklahoma State Highway Patrol ruled she fell asleep at the wheel. However, an independent investigation found dent marks on her rear bumper consistent with being rammed from behind, suggesting her car was deliberately forced off the road. The documents she was carrying were never found in the wreckage. Days before her death, her apartment had been deliberately contaminated with plutonium — from the Kerr-McGee plant — that she had no access to. Kerr-McGee settled a wrongful death lawsuit for $1.38 million without admitting liability.

## Circumstances of Death

On the evening of November 13, 1974, Silkwood attended a union meeting at the Hub Cafe in Crescent, Oklahoma. After the meeting, at approximately 7:00 PM, she left alone in her white Honda Civic, heading south on Highway 74 toward Oklahoma City, where she was scheduled to meet David Burnham, a reporter for the New York Times, and Steve Wodka, a union official from the Oil, Chemical, and Atomic Workers International Union.

Silkwood was carrying a brown manila folder and a large notebook containing documents and photographs she said would demonstrate the extent of safety problems at the Kerr-McGee Cimarron Fuel Fabrication Site. She had told colleagues the documents would prove the company was falsifying quality-control data for plutonium fuel rods.

Her car was found crashed into a concrete culvert wall approximately 7.3 miles from the Hub Cafe. She was dead at the scene. The Oklahoma State Highway Patrol concluded she had fallen asleep at the wheel, possibly under the influence of prescribed medication (methaqualone). A small amount of alcohol was also found in her system.

However, an independent accident investigation commissioned by the union found fresh dent marks and paint scratches on the left rear bumper of Silkwood's car, consistent with being struck from behind by another vehicle. This evidence contradicted the official "fell asleep" conclusion and suggested her car was deliberately forced off the road.

The documents Silkwood had been carrying were never found in the car wreckage.

## Background

Karen Silkwood was a chemical technician and laboratory analyst at the Kerr-McGee Corporation's Cimarron Fuel Fabrication Site near Crescent, Oklahoma, which manufactured plutonium fuel rods for nuclear reactors. She joined the plant in 1972 and was elected to the bargaining committee of the Oil, Chemical, and Atomic Workers International Union (OCAW) Local 5-283.

Through her union work, Silkwood became alarmed by what she witnessed: falsified quality-control records, workers handling radioactive materials without proper protection, cracked and defective fuel rods being approved for use, and management's systematic disregard for safety procedures. She testified before the U.S. Atomic Energy Commission (AEC) about unsafe conditions at the plant.

In the week before her death, Silkwood herself was found to be contaminated with extremely high levels of plutonium — up to 400 times the legal limit. Testing revealed that her apartment had also been deliberately contaminated with plutonium from the plant. Silkwood had no authorized access to plutonium outside the plant, and investigators never determined how the contamination occurred.
