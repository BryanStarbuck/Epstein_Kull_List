## Assessment: CONFIRMED

The European Court of Human Rights ruled in August 2019 that Russia was responsible for Magnitsky's death, finding that he was denied medical treatment for a life-threatening condition and was beaten by prison guards shortly before his death. The official death certificate listed "closed cerebral cranial injury" and a 2011 post-mortem found the death was caused by "traumatic application of blunt hard objects." His death led to the passage of the Magnitsky Act in the US and Global Magnitsky Acts in multiple countries.

## Circumstances of Death

Sergei Magnitsky was arrested on November 24, 2008, after testifying that Russian Interior Ministry officials had orchestrated a $230 million tax fraud. He was held in pretrial detention at Moscow's Butyrka prison — one of Russia's most overcrowded and unsanitary facilities.

During his 358 days in custody, Magnitsky developed severe abdominal pain diagnosed as gallstones, pancreatitis, and calculous cholecystitis. Despite repeated requests — documented in over 20 complaints filed by Magnitsky himself — he was systematically denied medical treatment. His requests to see a doctor were ignored or rejected. At one point, he was transferred to a facility with even worse conditions rather than to a hospital.

On November 16, 2009 — eight days before he would have had to be released if not brought to trial — Magnitsky's condition became critical. Instead of receiving medical care, he was allegedly placed in a cell with no bed, chained to a chair, and beaten by prison guards using rubber batons for over an hour. He died shortly thereafter.

The official Russian death certificate listed "closed cerebral cranial injury" as the cause of death. A post-mortem examination revealed numerous bruises and wounds on his legs and hands. A subsequent 2011 examination summarized the cause as "traumatic application of the blunt hard object (objects)" confirmed by "abrasions, ecchymomas, blood effusions into the soft tissues."

Russia posthumously put Magnitsky on trial for the very tax fraud he had exposed — convicting a dead man in July 2013 in proceedings widely condemned as a travesty of justice.

## Background

Sergei Magnitsky was a tax advisor at the Moscow law firm Firestone Duncan, working primarily for Hermitage Capital Management, once the largest foreign portfolio investor in Russia. In late 2007, Magnitsky and his colleagues discovered that corporate documents and company seals seized by Russian Interior Ministry officials in a June 2007 raid had been used to fraudulently re-register three Hermitage subsidiary companies.

The stolen companies were then used to file fraudulent tax returns, claiming refunds of $230 million — the largest tax refund in Russian history — paid out by the Russian Treasury on December 24, 2007. Magnitsky traced the fraud directly to officials within the Interior Ministry and the tax inspectorate.

In October 2008, Magnitsky testified to Russian investigators, naming specific officials as perpetrators. One month later, he was arrested — not the officials he had named, but Magnitsky himself, accused of the very fraud he had exposed.
