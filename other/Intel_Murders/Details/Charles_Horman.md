# Charles Horman
American journalist and filmmaker, executed by the Chilean military during the 1973 Pinochet coup. Declassified U.S. documents and a Chilean court ruling confirmed that U.S. intelligence played a "fundamental role" in his death. His story was the basis for the 1982 Costa-Gavras film *Missing*.

## Assessment: CONFIRMED

U.S. government complicity in Horman's death is confirmed by declassified documents and judicial findings. A 1976 State Department memo, declassified in 1999, concluded that the CIA may have had "an unfortunate part" in Horman's death. In 2014, a Chilean court ruled that U.S. military intelligence played a "fundamental role" in the murders of Horman and Frank Teruggi. In 2015, two former Chilean intelligence officials were convicted for the killings.

## Circumstances of Death

Horman was in Vina del Mar at the time of the September 11, 1973 coup and witnessed U.S. military personnel interacting closely with the Chilean junta forces. He returned to Santiago and was seized from his home by Chilean military forces on approximately September 17, 1973. He was taken to the National Stadium, which had been converted into a detention and execution center. He was executed around September 18, 1973. His body was not returned to his family until months later, and the Chilean government initially denied knowledge of his fate. His father, Edmund Horman, traveled to Chile to search for him and later sued former Secretary of State Henry Kissinger and other U.S. officials.

## Background

Horman was a Harvard-educated journalist and documentary filmmaker from New York. He moved to Chile in 1972 with his wife, Joyce, drawn by the social experiment of Salvador Allende's democratic socialist government. He worked as a freelance journalist and was producing a documentary about Chilean life. His time in Vina del Mar during the coup was particularly significant because he reportedly witnessed evidence of direct U.S. military involvement in the coup operations, which may have made him a target.
