## Assessment: HIGHLY SUSPICIOUS

Sakine Cansiz was a co-founder of the Kurdistan Workers' Party (PKK) who was shot execution-style along with [Fidan Doğan](Fidan_Dogan.md) and [Leyla Saylemez](Leyla_Saylemez.md) at the Kurdistan Information Center in Paris. The sole suspect, Ömer Güney, died of brain cancer before trial. Documents published by Turkish media, including what appeared to be a MIT "mission order" and recorded conversations between Güney and Turkish intelligence handlers, point to a state-directed assassination. French investigators reportedly concluded that Turkish intelligence was involved.

## Circumstances of Death

During the night of January 9-10, 2013, the three women were found dead inside the Kurdistan Information Center at 147 Rue La Fayette in the 10th arrondissement of Paris. Each had been shot multiple times in the head and neck at close range — execution-style. The building's security camera footage showed suspect Ömer Güney entering the premises between 00:11 and 00:56, the time frame in which the killings occurred. There were no signs of forced entry, suggesting the killer was known to the victims.

## Background

Sakine Cansiz was one of the founding members of the PKK in 1978 alongside Abdullah Öcalan. She spent years in Turkish prisons in the 1980s and 1990s, where she reportedly endured severe torture. After her release, she became a prominent figure in the Kurdish diaspora movement in Europe, operating from France and Germany. She was a symbol of Kurdish women's resistance and played a key role in the PKK's women's movement. At the time of her murder, peace negotiations between the Turkish government and the PKK were reportedly underway.

## Intelligence Connections

* Turkish media published a recording of a conversation allegedly between Ömer Güney and two Turkish intelligence (MIT) handlers
* A document resembling a MIT "mission order" was reportedly leaked, allegedly authorizing the operation
* French investigators reportedly concluded that members of MIT were involved in the triple assassination
* The families of the victims filed legal complaints in 2017 with documents they said proved the killings were "an operation carefully planned by the Turkish secret services"
* MIT officially denied any involvement in 2014
* Suspect Ömer Güney died of brain cancer in December 2016 in a Paris prison, just days before his trial was set to begin — raising questions about the timing of his death

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- Three women shot execution-style with multiple bullets to the head indicates professional assassination, not random violence
- The sole suspect died of brain cancer days before his trial — preventing any testimony that might have revealed his handlers
- Documents and recordings allegedly linking MIT to the operation surfaced in Turkish media
- The killings occurred during a sensitive period of Turkish-PKK peace negotiations, potentially aimed at sabotaging the talks
- The Paris prosecutor initially closed the case after Güney's death, but an anti-terror judge was later appointed after the families filed complaints
- No other suspects have been charged despite the evidence allegedly pointing to a broader intelligence operation
