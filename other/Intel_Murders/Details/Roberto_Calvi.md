## Assessment: HIGHLY SUSPICIOUS

Roberto Calvi's death was ruled a murder after two coroners' inquests and an independent forensic investigation. The first inquest in 1982 ruled suicide, but the second in 1983 returned an open verdict. Forensic scientist Angela Gallop proved that Calvi could not have walked on the scaffolding himself because no paint or rust was found on his shoes. Five people were acquitted of his murder in 2007, and the case remains officially unsolved.

## Circumstances of Death

On the morning of June 18, 1982, a postal clerk crossing Blackfriars Bridge at 7:30 AM discovered Calvi's body hanging from scaffolding beneath the bridge. He had five bricks stuffed in his pockets and was carrying approximately $14,000 in three different currencies. An orange rope was tied around his neck and attached to the scaffolding.

Calvi had fled Italy on June 10, traveling via Venice and Austria to London using a false passport. He had been convicted of fraud in Italy and was facing a massive financial scandal at Banco Ambrosiano.

## Background

Calvi was an Italian banker who served as chairman of Banco Ambrosiano, Italy's largest private bank. He was dubbed "God's Banker" by the press because of his close financial dealings with the Vatican Bank (Istituto per le Opere di Religione, or IOR). The Vatican Bank was the largest shareholder in Banco Ambrosiano.

Calvi was a member of Propaganda Due (P2), an illegal and influential masonic lodge led by Licio Gelli. P2 members referred to themselves as "Black Friars" -- a detail that has led to speculation that Calvi was murdered as a masonic warning, given the symbolism of his body being found at Blackfriars Bridge.

In the weeks before his death, Banco Ambrosiano collapsed with debts of $1.3 billion, much of which had been funneled through shell companies in Latin America connected to the Vatican Bank. Calvi's secretary, Graziella Corrocher, jumped to her death from the bank's fifth-floor window on the same day, leaving a note blaming Calvi.

## Intelligence Connections

- Calvi was a member of Licio Gelli's P2 lodge, which included senior Italian intelligence (SISMI) officers, military commanders, politicians, and mafia figures
- P2 has been described as a "state within a state" with deep connections to Italian intelligence and NATO's Operation Gladio stay-behind network
- The Vatican Bank's financial dealings with Banco Ambrosiano allegedly involved laundering money for Italian intelligence operations
- Calvi reportedly told his wife: "I have seen the list of 120 things Gelli has on his file for Italian politicians. Both in the Banco Ambrosiano and in P2, I was the person who carried out the orders"
- The Sicilian Mafia allegedly used Banco Ambrosiano to launder drug money through Vatican accounts
- Calvi had written letters to Pope John Paul II warning that the collapse of Banco Ambrosiano would create a scandal that could "provoke a catastrophe of unimaginable proportions"

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- Forensic investigation proved Calvi could not have hanged himself: no paint or rust on his shoes from walking on scaffolding
- The bricks in his pockets and the location (Blackfriars Bridge) suggested a ritualistic or symbolic killing
- P2 members called themselves "Black Friars" -- the bridge location may have been a deliberate message
- Calvi's briefcase, allegedly containing documents implicating powerful figures, was never recovered
- Five people (including Mafia boss Pippo Calo and P2 financier Flavio Carboni) were charged but acquitted in 2007
- The Vatican Bank eventually paid $244 million to creditors in a deal that acknowledged no wrongdoing
- Calvi's family consistently maintained he was murdered and rejected the initial suicide ruling
- His secretary's suicide on the same day adds to the suspicious circumstances
