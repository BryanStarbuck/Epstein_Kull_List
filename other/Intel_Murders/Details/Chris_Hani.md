## Circumstances of Death

On the morning of April 10, 1993, Chris Hani returned to his home in the Dawn Park suburb of Boksburg, east of Johannesburg. As he stepped out of his car, Janusz Walus approached and shot him in the head and back with a Z88 pistol. Hani died at the scene. A neighbor, Retha Harmse, witnessed the shooting and noted the license plate of the fleeing vehicle, leading to Walus's rapid arrest. The murder weapon was traced to Clive Derby-Lewis, a Conservative Party MP, who had supplied it.

## Background

Chris Hani was one of the most popular political figures in South Africa and was widely seen as a potential successor to Nelson Mandela. He joined the ANC Youth League as a teenager and became a member of the South African Communist Party. He rose to become chief of staff of Umkhonto we Sizwe (MK), the ANC's armed wing, and later General Secretary of the SACP. His assassination came at a critical moment in South Africa's transition from apartheid, when negotiations for a democratic constitution were underway. The killing nearly derailed the entire peace process and triggered widespread unrest.

## Aftermath

Hani's assassination triggered massive protests across South Africa and nearly derailed the negotiations that would lead to the country's first democratic elections. Nelson Mandela went on national television to appeal for calm, in what many consider the moment he effectively became the nation's leader. The killing ultimately accelerated the transition, as both sides recognized the urgency of reaching a settlement. The first democratic elections were held on April 27, 1994, one year after Hani's death. Walus was paroled in November 2022 after serving nearly 30 years and was deported to Poland. Hani remains one of the most revered figures in South African political history.
