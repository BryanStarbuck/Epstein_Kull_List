## Assessment: HIGHLY SUSPICIOUS

Rick Turner was one of apartheid South Africa's most influential dissident intellectuals. He was shot through the front door of his home by an unknown assailant who was never identified. The bullet matched ammunition used by the South African police. The apartheid government's police minister had reportedly called Turner "the most dangerous man in South Africa." Despite widespread belief that the security services were responsible, no one has ever been charged.

## Circumstances of Death

Shortly after midnight on January 8, 1978, Rick Turner answered a knock at the front door of his suburban Durban home. He was shot through the door with a 9mm bullet. Turner died in the arms of his 13-year-old daughter, Jann. Despite months of police investigation, no significant clues were reported and no suspects were identified. The case has never been solved.

## Background

Richard Turner was born in Stellenbosch in 1941 and studied philosophy at the University of Paris (Sorbonne) under Henri Lefebvre, earning a doctorate for a dissertation on Jean-Paul Sartre. He returned to South Africa and became a senior lecturer in political science at the University of Natal in Durban, where in 1970 he met and formed a close relationship with Steve Biko. In 1972, he published *The Eye of the Needle: A Guide to Participatory Democracy in South Africa*, a visionary work imagining a decentralized socialist post-apartheid society. In 1973, the apartheid government banned him for five years — severely restricting his movement, associations, and ability to publish. Under the banning order, he could not leave Durban, could not be quoted in media, and could not attend gatherings. He was killed before the banning order expired.
