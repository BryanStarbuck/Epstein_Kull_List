# Victor Jara
Chilean folk singer, poet, theater director, and political activist, tortured and murdered by Chilean military forces during the 1973 Pinochet coup. His hands were crushed and he was shot 44 times in Chile Stadium — a killing that became one of the most infamous atrocities of the Chilean dictatorship.

## Assessment: CONFIRMED

Jara's murder by the Chilean military during the September 11, 1973 coup is thoroughly documented. In 2018, eight retired Chilean military officers were sentenced to 15 years in prison for his murder. In 2016, a U.S. federal jury found former Chilean Army officer Pedro Barrientos civilly liable for the killing. Barrientos was extradited from the United States to Chile in December 2023 to face criminal charges. The coup itself was supported by the CIA and the Nixon administration.

## Circumstances of Death

On September 12, 1973, the day after the coup, Jara was arrested at the State Technical University where he worked as a professor. As a famous musician and prominent supporter of President Salvador Allende, he was immediately recognized. He was taken to Chile Stadium (Estadio Chile), which had been converted into a mass detention and torture center. Over the following days, Jara was singled out for particularly brutal treatment. According to multiple witness accounts, soldiers crushed his fingers and smashed his hands with rifle butts — a deliberate symbolic act to silence his guitar playing. An officer reportedly threw a lit cigarette on the ground, made Jara crawl for it, then stomped on his wrists. He was then shot 44 times and his body was dumped on the street of a Santiago shantytown. His body was later identified at the city morgue by his wife, Joan Jara.

## Background

Jara was one of Chile's most beloved folk musicians and a leading figure in the Nueva Cancion Chilena (New Chilean Song) movement, which blended traditional folk music with social and political themes. He was also a Communist Party member and a vocal supporter of President Allende's socialist government. His music — including songs like "Te Recuerdo Amanda" and "El Derecho de Vivir en Paz" — had made him a cultural symbol of the Chilean left. His prominence made him a prime target for the military junta that overthrew Allende.
