## Assessment: HIGHLY SUSPICIOUS

Gerald Bull was shot five times at point-blank range outside his apartment in Brussels while working on Project Babylon, a "supergun" for Saddam Hussein's Iraq. The assassination bears all the hallmarks of a professional intelligence operation. Mossad is the primary suspect, with investigative journalists reporting that Israeli Prime Minister Yitzhak Shamir sanctioned the killing. No person has ever been charged.

## Circumstances of Death

On the evening of March 22, 1990, Bull returned to his apartment at 2 Place Guy d'Arezzo in the Uccle municipality of Brussels. As he approached his door, an assassin shot him five times -- three times in the head and twice in the back -- using a 7.65mm pistol with a silencer. His body was found by his neighbors. Nothing was stolen from his apartment, and $20,000 in cash was left untouched, ruling out robbery.

## Background

Bull was a Canadian engineer who was a prodigious talent in ballistics and aeronautics. He earned his PhD in aeronautical engineering from the University of Toronto at the age of 24. He worked for the Canadian Armament and Research Development Establishment (CARDE) and later became a professor at McGill University.

Bull's lifelong obsession was developing a method to launch satellites into orbit using massive artillery pieces rather than rockets. He worked on various projects including HARP (High Altitude Research Project) with the US Army and Canadian government. After funding dried up, Bull turned to selling artillery technology to South Africa (during apartheid) and was convicted in the US in 1980 for arms dealing, serving six months in prison.

After his release, Bull moved to Brussels and began working with Iraq's government on Project Babylon -- a "supergun" with a barrel over 150 meters long, theoretically capable of launching projectiles into orbit or hitting targets at extreme range. He also designed modified long-range artillery for Iraq, including the GC-45 howitzer.

## Intelligence Connections

- According to investigative journalist Gordon Thomas, Mossad chief Nahum Admoni sent a three-man assassination team to Brussels, and the killing was sanctioned by Israeli Prime Minister Yitzhak Shamir
- Bull had previously worked with CIA support on artillery projects, including the HARP program
- He had worked with South African intelligence on artillery development during apartheid
- Israeli intelligence reportedly warned Bull multiple times to stop his work for Iraq before the assassination
- The CIA was aware of Bull's work for Iraq and reportedly debriefed him periodically
- MI6 also had interest in monitoring Bull's activities given British involvement in Iraq arms deals (the "Supergun Affair" later exposed in the UK)
- Iran, Syria, Chile, and South Africa have all been suggested as alternative suspects

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- Professional execution: five shots at point-blank range with a silenced weapon, nothing stolen
- Multiple intelligence agencies had motive: Mossad (preventing Iraqi superweapon), CIA (possible double-cross), MI6, Iran
- Bull had reportedly received warnings from Mossad to stop his Iraq work but continued
- The timing came just months before Iraq's invasion of Kuwait, when intelligence agencies were closely monitoring Iraqi weapons programs
- After Bull's death, Project Babylon components were seized by British customs, and the project collapsed
- PBS Frontline produced a documentary "The Man Who Made the Supergun" examining the case
- Despite clear intelligence motives and a professional execution, no one has ever been charged
