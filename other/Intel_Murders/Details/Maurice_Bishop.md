## Assessment: SUSPICIOUS

While Bishop was directly killed by a Marxist-Leninist faction within his own party, the broader context raises questions about CIA destabilization. The United States had long sought to undermine Bishop's People's Revolutionary Government, and the internal coup provided the pretext for a full-scale U.S. military invasion just six days later. Declassified documents show the Reagan administration was actively planning intervention in Grenada before the coup. Bishop's remains have never been found, and U.S. government documents suggest American forces played a role in recovering and possibly disposing of the remains.

## Circumstances of Death

On October 13, 1983, Bishop was placed under house arrest by forces loyal to Deputy Prime Minister Bernard Coard, who demanded a power-sharing arrangement. Six days later, on October 19, a crowd of thousands of supporters freed Bishop and marched to Fort Rupert (now Fort George) in the capital, St. George's. Soldiers loyal to Coard and General Hudson Austin stormed the fort with armored vehicles. Bishop, along with cabinet ministers Jacqueline Creft (who was pregnant), Unison Whiteman, Norris Bain, and Fitzroy Bain, were lined up against a wall and executed by machine gun fire. A Revolutionary Military Council under Hudson Austin then took power, imposing a shoot-on-sight curfew.

## Background

Maurice Bishop was born on the island of Aruba to Grenadian parents and grew up in Grenada. He studied law in London, where he was influenced by the Black Power and anti-colonial movements. Returning to Grenada, he co-founded the New Jewel Movement (NJM — Joint Endeavour for Welfare, Education, and Liberation) in 1973. On March 13, 1979, the NJM overthrew the corrupt government of Eric Gairy in a nearly bloodless coup and established the People's Revolutionary Government (PRG) with Bishop as Prime Minister. Bishop pursued social programs in literacy, healthcare, and women's rights, and built close ties with Cuba, which helped construct a new international airport. These ties alarmed the Reagan administration, which viewed Grenada as a Cuban-Soviet foothold in the Caribbean.

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- The internal coup conveniently provided the Reagan administration with a pretext for military intervention it had already been planning
- Bishop's remains have never been found despite extensive searches, with suspicion falling on U.S. forces
- The U.S. invasion came just six days after Bishop's execution, suggesting pre-positioned military readiness
- The speed of the American military response (Operation Urgent Fury, October 25, 1983) implies advance planning beyond what the immediate crisis warranted
- The invasion occurred just two days after the Beirut barracks bombing killed 241 U.S. Marines, and some analysts suggest Grenada served as a distraction
- Fourteen of the coup plotters, including Coard, were sentenced to death (later commuted), but the full story of who encouraged the internal split remains murky
