## Assessment: SUSPICIOUS

Despite decades of investigation involving over 10,000 interviews and 134 confessions, Palme's assassination remains effectively unsolved. The case was officially closed in 2020 with prosecutors naming Stig Engstrom ("the Skandia Man") as the most likely suspect, but Engstrom died in 2000 and was never charged or tried. Multiple intelligence services had motive: South African intelligence (Palme supported the ANC), CIA and MI6 (Palme was neutral in the Cold War and critical of US policy), and Stay-Behind networks. Swedish CID chief Tommy Lindstrom identified apartheid South Africa as the number one suspect.

## Circumstances of Death

On February 28, 1986, at 23:21 CET, Palme was walking home from the Grand Cinema on Sveavagen, one of Stockholm's main streets, with his wife Lisbet. They had decided to go to the cinema spontaneously and without bodyguards -- a privilege Palme insisted upon as part of Sweden's open democratic culture.

A gunman approached from behind and fired a single shot at close range that struck Palme in the back, passing through his body. A second shot grazed Lisbet Palme in the back. Palme collapsed on the sidewalk and was pronounced dead on arrival at Sabbatsberg Hospital at 00:06 on March 1.

The killer fled up the steps of Tunnelgatan toward the Brunkebergs ridge and disappeared.

## Background

Olof Palme was a towering figure in European social democracy who served as Prime Minister of Sweden from 1969-1976 and again from 1982 until his death. He was one of the most outspoken Western leaders during the Cold War, criticizing both US involvement in Vietnam and Soviet repression.

Palme was a strong supporter of the African National Congress (ANC) and provided secret financial support to the anti-apartheid movement through channels routed through Switzerland. He was vocal in condemning South Africa's apartheid regime.

## Why This Death Raises Questions

- The world's longest-running murder investigation (34 years) ended without a trial
- The named suspect, Stig Engstrom, died by suicide in 2000 and was never tested in court
- The evidence against Engstrom was entirely circumstantial and "too weak to ensure a trial"
- The initial investigation was chaotically mishandled: the crime scene was not properly secured, and evidence was contaminated
- Christer Pettersson was convicted in 1988 based on Lisbet Palme's identification, but acquitted on appeal
- Multiple intelligence services had motive to remove Palme
- Sweden's own police and security services have been accused of obstructing the investigation
- The 2020 conclusion was widely criticized by lawyers, police, and journalists
